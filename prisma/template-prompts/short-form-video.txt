GOAL
Create a short-form video that communicates one clear idea, holds attention through relevance and pacing, and leads naturally to one viewer action.
The video should feel made for {{platform}}, not like a long article compressed into a vertical video.
ACCURACY

Use only facts, examples, results, quotations, statistics, and experiences supplied in the prompt.
Do not invent personal stories, customer results, studies, numbers, conversations, credentials, or professional achievements.
Do not imply that the creator personally experienced something unless it appears in {{personalExperience}}.
Do not invent product features, prices, guarantees, discounts, deadlines, or outcomes.
Clearly distinguish opinion, interpretation, and recommendation from confirmed fact.
Do not make guaranteed health, financial, career, business, relationship, or performance claims.
If information is uncertain, use measured language instead of presenting it as confirmed.
PLATFORM ADAPTATION
Adapt the script to the conventions of {{platform}} and {{videoFormat}}.
Consider:


expected pacing;
typical viewer context;
whether the video is watched with sound off;
whether direct-to-camera delivery is appropriate;
caption visibility;
safe text placement;
platform-specific audience expectations;
whether the goal is retention, education, comments, traffic, leads, or sales.
Do not automatically use the same pacing, hook, or CTA for TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, LinkedIn, and other platforms.
DURATION AND PACING
Build the timing proportionally around {{duration}} rather than assuming a 60-second video.
Use this approximate structure:
Opening hook: first 5–10% of the total duration.
Context and setup: next 10–20%.
Core insight or development: approximately 50–65%.
Takeaway and CTA: final 10–20%.
Adjust these proportions when the topic requires a demonstration, story, comparison, or explanation.
Estimate spoken length based on a natural delivery speed of approximately 130–160 words per minute.
Use a slower pace for:
emotional topics;
complex explanations;
serious subjects;
unfamiliar terminology.
Use a faster pace only when it remains understandable.
Do not write more spoken text than can be delivered naturally within {{duration}} seconds.
HOOK
Create a visual and verbal hook for the opening seconds.
The hook should:
immediately establish the topic or tension;
appeal specifically to {{audience}};
introduce a useful promise, problem, contrast, observation, or question;
accurately represent what the video delivers;
be understandable without a long introduction.
Possible hook approaches include:
a recognizable situation;
a specific contradiction;
an unexpected but supported fact;
a direct demonstration;
a result shown before the explanation;
a clear audience-specific question;
a strong opinion supported by the rest of the video.
Avoid empty or misleading hooks such as:
“Stop scrolling”
“Nobody is talking about this”
“This changes everything”
“You need to hear this”
“The secret they don’t want you to know”
“Watch until the end”
“You’ve been doing this wrong your whole life”
“This will make you rich”
“This will change your life”
“99% of people don’t know this” unless verified.
The visual hook and verbal hook should support each other rather than communicate two unrelated ideas.
CORE CONTENT
Build the video around one central insight.
Depending on the topic, use one of these structures:
problem → explanation → practical takeaway;
misconception → correction → example;
before → change → after;
question → answer → implication;
demonstration → explanation → next step;
mistake → consequence → better approach;
short story → realization → lesson;
claim → evidence → conclusion.
Do not force every video into a list of three tips.
Do not introduce several unrelated ideas.
Every sentence should either:
advance the explanation;
show an example;
increase clarity;
create a meaningful transition;
support the final takeaway.
Remove background information that the viewer does not need.
SCENES AND EDITING
Divide the script into clear time-coded segments based on {{duration}}.
For each segment provide:
approximate time range;
spoken dialogue or voiceover;
what appears visually;
on-screen text;
editing or transition cue;
optional B-roll or sound cue.
Use jump cuts only when they suit {{editingStyle}}, {{tone}}, and {{platform}}.
Do not add a jump cut after every sentence.
Use editing changes to:
remove pauses;
emphasize a transition;
introduce an example;
change perspective;
maintain visual clarity.
Possible visual changes include:
direct-to-camera shot;
close-up reaction;
screen recording;
product demonstration;
object detail;
simple text card;
relevant B-roll;
before-and-after comparison;
change of camera distance;
change of location;
graphic or diagram.
Do not suggest footage, locations, props, interfaces, or production capabilities that are unavailable according to {{productionLimitations}}.
ON-SCREEN TEXT
Use short on-screen text to reinforce key information.
On-screen text should:
be understandable at a glance;
highlight the main phrase, number, step, or contrast;
remain readable on a mobile screen;
avoid covering the speaker’s face or key visual;
use the same terminology as the spoken script;
remain inside platform-safe areas.
Do not place the full spoken script on screen unless {{captionStyle}} requires full subtitles.
Do not repeat every sentence as a separate text card.
Keep most on-screen text to approximately 2–8 words.
Use brackets in the script:
[ON-SCREEN TEXT: concise phrase]
Use a separate subtitle track recommendation when full captions are needed.
NATURAL SPOKEN LANGUAGE
Write for speech, not for an article.
Use:
natural contractions when appropriate;
short spoken sentences;
clear transitions;
pronunciation-friendly wording;
varied rhythm;
occasional pauses for emphasis.
Avoid:
formal written language;
long nested sentences;
dense lists;
excessive terminology;
corporate jargon;
artificial enthusiasm;
repeated rhetorical questions;
saying the same idea in both the hook and conclusion;
unnatural phrases written only to contain keywords.
Read the dialogue mentally as spoken language and simplify any line that would sound awkward aloud.
CTA
End with one CTA that matches {{goal}} and {{desiredAction}}.
The CTA should usually occupy no more than the final 10–15% of the video.
Possible CTA types include:
ask one specific question;
invite viewers to try one step;
encourage saving a genuinely reusable guide;
direct viewers to a supplied link;
invite viewers to read a related resource;
ask viewers to compare their experience;
request a comment with a specific answer;
introduce a product or service transparently;
encourage following for a clearly defined content theme.
Do not ask viewers to like, comment, save, share, follow, subscribe, and click at the same time.
Do not use “Follow for more” as the default CTA.
Do not add a link, discount, offer, or free resource unless it was supplied.
VISUAL AND AUDIO GUIDANCE
Suggest practical production choices.
Visual guidance should:
match the subject and available resources;
be achievable within the stated setup;
avoid unnecessary cinematic complexity;
support the spoken point;
provide visual variation without distracting from the message.
Audio guidance should:
prioritize clear speech;
avoid music that competes with dialogue;
use sound effects sparingly;
mention silence or a pause when it strengthens a moment;
avoid recommending copyrighted music or specific trending audio unless supplied.
ACCESSIBILITY
Assume some viewers will watch without sound.
Include:
readable subtitles;
sufficient visual context;
concise on-screen emphasis;
clear contrast;
no essential meaning communicated through audio alone.
Do not rely only on color to distinguish concepts.
STYLE
Write in a natural, platform-appropriate voice.
Match {{tone}}, {{brandVoice}}, and {{creatorRole}}.
Avoid:
generic motivational speeches;
forced relatability;
fake vulnerability;
exaggerated reactions;
excessive slang;
constant shouting or high-energy delivery;
unnecessary controversy;
emotional manipulation;
repetitive sentence fragments;
several hooks inside one video;
unnatural pauses after every line.
Avoid common AI and short-form clichés such as:
“Here’s the thing”
“Let that sink in”
“It’s not about X, it’s about Y”
“Game-changer”
“Unlock your potential”
“Your future self will thank you”
“I wish I knew this sooner” unless true;
“You’re welcome”
“Thank me later”
“Save this before it disappears”
“Part two if this blows up”
FINAL QUALITY CHECK
Before presenting the script, silently verify that:
the timing fits {{duration}};
the spoken word count is realistic;
the video communicates one central insight;
the hook accurately represents the content;
no fact, result, story, product feature, or statistic was invented;
the language sounds natural when spoken;
the visual instructions are achievable;
editing cues support rather than overwhelm the content;
on-screen text is concise;
the video works with subtitles and without sound;
the CTA is brief and connected to the goal;
the script does not sound like a generic AI-generated video.
