GOAL
Write a credible case study showing:
the client’s situation before using the product;
why the problem mattered;
what was actually implemented;
how the implementation worked;
which verified outcomes followed;
what a similar reader can realistically learn from the example.
The case study should feel evidence-based and specific, not like a fictional success story or exaggerated sales page.
ACCURACY AND EVIDENCE


Use only facts, metrics, timelines, processes, quotations, and outcomes supplied in the prompt.
Do not invent percentages, revenue figures, time savings, conversion improvements, customer counts, implementation dates, employee numbers, or product usage details.
Do not invent actions, meetings, conversations, objections, or decisions made by the client.
Do not create a customer quote when an approved quote was not supplied.
Do not imply that the product alone caused the result unless the available evidence supports that conclusion.
When other factors contributed to the result, acknowledge them briefly.
Do not describe correlation as proven causation.
Do not use estimated numbers as confirmed metrics.
Clearly distinguish:
   * verified quantitative results;
   * qualitative feedback;
   * estimates;
   * observations;
   * outcomes still being measured.
Do not claim that another customer will achieve the same result.
Do not add awards, certifications, integrations, features, or customer information that was not supplied.
Respect all confidentiality restrictions.
If the client cannot be named, replace identifying details with an accurate description such as “a mid-sized European e-commerce company.”
Do not disclose private operational, financial, security, employee, or customer data.
CLIENT PERMISSION
Use {{clientName}} only when {{clientPermission}} confirms that the client may be named publicly.
If permission is absent or unclear:


anonymize the client;
remove identifying information;
do not use the client’s logo;
do not present an unapproved quote as public testimony.
HEADLINE
Write a clear, specific headline focused on the verified outcome.
The headline should:
identify the client or client type;
mention the product or relevant solution when useful;
highlight the strongest confirmed result;
avoid exaggerated promises;
avoid claiming causation that has not been established.
Do not use vague headlines such as:
“A Story of Transformation”
“How Innovation Changed Everything”
“An Incredible Success Story”
“Revolutionizing the Industry”
“From Struggle to Success”
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Write exactly three concise sentences:


Identify the client and the business problem.
Explain what solution was implemented.
State the strongest verified outcome and relevant measurement period.
Do not introduce information that is not explained later in the case study.
THE CHALLENGE
Explain:


the client’s situation before implementation;
the specific operational or business problem;
why the problem mattered;
who was affected;
the limitations of the previous approach;
what the client needed from a new solution.
Use concrete details when supplied.
Do not exaggerate the severity of the challenge.
Do not make the client appear incompetent in order to make the product look stronger.
THE SOLUTION
Explain:
why the client selected {{productName}};
which features or services were actually used;
how implementation occurred;
which teams were involved;
how the product fit into the client’s existing process;
any relevant setup, training, integration, or customization;
implementation challenges and how they were handled.
Focus on the practical process rather than listing every product feature.
Do not describe unused features.
Do not imply that implementation was instant or effortless unless that is confirmed.
THE RESULTS
Present the outcomes with sufficient context.
For every quantitative result, include when available:
the starting baseline;
the final measurement;
the measurement period;
the method used;
whether the result was directly measured or estimated.
Include up to three metric bullets only when three verified metrics were supplied.
If fewer than three verified metrics are available, use only the confirmed metrics and add qualitative outcomes separately. Never invent additional metrics to complete the format.
Use this metric style:
**[Metric or result]:** [specific change], measured over [timeframe] using [method or data source].
Possible result categories include:
time saved;
cost reduction;
revenue or conversion change;
error reduction;
processing speed;
employee workload;
customer satisfaction;
adoption;
completion rate;
operational consistency.
Use only categories supported by the supplied evidence.
CONTEXT AND LIMITATIONS
Briefly mention relevant qualifications, such as:
the result applies to a specific team, region, workflow, or period;
implementation was combined with process changes or training;
some outcomes are still being measured;
results may not be directly transferable to every organization.
This section should add credibility without unnecessarily weakening a well-supported result.
CUSTOMER QUOTE
Use the approved quote exactly as supplied, except for minor grammar or punctuation corrections that do not change its meaning.
Format:
“[Approved customer quote]”
— [Name, role, company]
If no approved quote was supplied, write:
[Customer quote pending approval]
Do not generate a fictional testimonial.
Do not invent a speaker name or job title.
CTA
End with one CTA that matches {{goal}} and {{desiredAction}}.
The CTA should tell the reader exactly what the next step is.
Possible CTA labels include:
“See how the product works”
“Request a tailored demo”
“Explore the relevant solution”
“Discuss your current workflow”
“View product details”
“Read another customer story”
Do not promise the same result achieved by the featured client.
Avoid vague CTA language such as:
“Learn more”
“Start your transformation”
“Unlock your potential”
“Get results now”
STYLE
Write in a clear, professional, evidence-based tone.
Use plain language, specific details, and varied sentence lengths.
Keep the client at the center of the story. The product should appear as part of the solution, not as the hero of an advertisement.
Avoid:
exaggerated praise;
unsupported superlatives;
dramatic storytelling;
excessive brand repetition;
long feature lists;
fake quotations;
generic business jargon;
guaranteed outcomes;
unnecessary exclamation marks;
describing every implementation as seamless.
Avoid phrases such as:
“game-changing solution”
“revolutionary platform”
“seamless implementation”
“unprecedented success”
“remarkable transformation”
“skyrocketed”
“changed everything”
“unlocking new levels of growth”
“in today’s competitive landscape”
“the results speak for themselves”
FINAL QUALITY CHECK
Before presenting the case study, silently verify that:
the client may legally and publicly be identified;
all confidential information has been excluded;
every number is supported by supplied evidence;
the baseline and measurement period are included where available;
no quote, result, timeline, event, or process was invented;
correlation is not presented as proven causation;
no more than the available verified metrics are shown;
the client’s challenge is described respectfully;
the implementation process is realistic;
limitations and contributing factors are acknowledged when relevant;
the CTA does not promise identical results;
the text sounds like a credible customer story rather than an advertisement.
