Create a polished, client-facing sales proposal that is persuasive because it is specific, credible, easy to evaluate, and commercially clear—not because it uses hype or pressure.

Use the supplied facts to explain the client’s situation, define the proposed solution, describe the work and commercial terms, reduce decision friction, and lead naturally to the requested next step.

HOW TO HANDLE INPUTS

1. Treat supplied facts as the only approved factual basis. Do not invent details to make the proposal look more complete.
2. Use blank optional fields intelligently. Omit sections or details that would be unsupported, combine adjacent sections when that improves flow, and choose a neutral professional default when a stylistic preference is blank.
3. Never write “not provided,” “N/A,” or expose raw placeholders in client-facing copy. If a missing fact is essential to final approval—such as a price, date, named deliverable, legal term, or measurable claim—insert a concise bracketed completion marker such as [Confirm final price] only where the user must supply it. Keep such markers rare.
4. If {{includePricing}} is false, omit pricing, payment, tax, discount, and validity details. Do not imply that pricing is attached or available elsewhere unless the inputs say so.
5. If {{includePricing}} is true, use only the supplied commercial figures and terms. Preserve numbers, currencies, billing periods, ranges, and tax wording exactly enough to avoid changing their commercial meaning.
6. Treat {{referencesAndSources}} as supporting material, not permission to fabricate a citation. Mention or footnote a source only when the input provides a usable source name, link, document, or attribution.
7. When the inputs conflict, prioritize explicit commercial facts and restrictions in {{additionalRequirements}} and {{commercialNotes}}, then scope details, then stylistic preferences. Do not silently reconcile contradictory prices, dates, deliverables, or obligations; flag the conflict in the final verification notes.

PROPOSAL STRATEGY

* Write for the people identified in {{decisionAudience}} and {{stakeholdersAndRoles}}. Make the proposal understandable to a mixed buying group without flattening important technical or commercial detail.
* Align the argument with {{proposalGoal}}, {{decisionCriteria}}, and {{positioningPriority}}. Emphasize the decision factors the client is likely to use, but do not overstate certainty.
* Show that the provider understands the client by connecting {{clientSituation}} and {{clientChallenges}} to the proposed work. Avoid repeating the client’s problem in several sections.
* Translate features, activities, and deliverables into practical client value. Do not promise an outcome that depends on variables outside the provider’s control.
* Address {{objectionsAndRisks}} calmly. Use scope clarity, assumptions, phased delivery, governance, evidence, or a pilot where supported. Do not use fear, disparage competitors, or imply that alternatives are incompetent.
* If {{competitorsOrAlternatives}} is supplied, position the offer through relevant differences and trade-offs. Do not make unverifiable superiority claims or invent competitor weaknesses.
* Adapt to {{relationshipStage}}. A proposal after discovery may be more specific and refer to known priorities; a new-prospect proposal should avoid pretending that discussions or agreements occurred; an RFP response should be structured and explicit; a renewal or expansion proposal should distinguish new value from existing work.

CLIENT-FACING STRUCTURE

Create the following sections in a natural order. Include optional sections only when supported by the inputs or requested in {{outputFeatures}}.

1. COVER OR TITLE BLOCK

Include a concise proposal title built around {{offerName}}, the names of {{providerName}} and {{clientName}}, and the proposal date only if a reliable date is available in the inputs. Do not invent a date. If “Cover page” is not requested, use a compact title block instead of a separate cover.

2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Write a decision-oriented summary that states the client context, the intended outcome, the proposed solution, the main value, and the requested next step. Keep it specific and readable. Do not reduce the entire proposal to slogans.

3. CLIENT CONTEXT AND PRIORITIES

Summarize the current situation, challenges, desired outcomes, decision criteria, and relevant market context. Demonstrate understanding without claiming inside knowledge beyond {{clientSituation}}, {{clientChallenges}}, {{desiredOutcomes}}, and {{industryContext}}.

4. PROPOSED SOLUTION

Explain what {{offerName}} is, how it responds to the client’s priorities, and why the proposed approach is appropriate. Use {{offerOverview}}, {{approachAndMethod}}, {{positioningPriority}}, and {{differentiators}}. Separate confirmed capabilities from anticipated benefits.

5. SCOPE AND DELIVERABLES

Present the included work and deliverables clearly. Use a table only when it improves comparison or scanning. When deliverables are broad, group them into logical workstreams. Do not invent quantities, formats, service levels, revision rounds, or acceptance criteria.

6. DELIVERY APPROACH, PHASES, AND TIMELINE

Describe the working method, phases, milestones, timeline, and proposed start date. If exact dates are absent, use relative sequencing rather than invented calendar dates. Make dependencies visible. Include an “Implementation table” only if requested in {{outputFeatures}} or clearly useful.

7. ROLES AND RESPONSIBILITIES

Clarify what {{providerName}} will do and what {{clientName}} must provide, approve, or make available. Use {{clientResponsibilities}} and supported scope information. Do not assign responsibilities that were not supplied or logically required by the stated work.

8. ASSUMPTIONS AND EXCLUSIONS

State assumptions and exclusions in neutral, commercially clear language. These sections should prevent ambiguity, not read like a defensive legal disclaimer. Omit either section when no supported content exists.

9. INVESTMENT AND COMMERCIAL TERMS

Include this section only when {{includePricing}} is true. Present the pricing model, amounts, currency, tax treatment, payment schedule, optional add-ons, validity date, and commercial notes. Distinguish required fees from optional items. Do not calculate totals unless the supplied information is sufficient and unambiguous. Do not create discounts, savings percentages, financing terms, or deadlines.

10. WHY THIS PROVIDER

Use {{differentiators}} and {{proofAndCaseExamples}} to support credibility. Approved examples may be summarized, but preserve their meaning and attribution. If evidence is limited, keep this section brief and focus on relevant method or fit rather than generic claims such as “industry-leading.”

11. RISKS, DEPENDENCIES, OR MITIGATIONS

Include a concise risk table only when requested in {{outputFeatures}} or when {{objectionsAndRisks}}, {{assumptions}}, or {{clientResponsibilities}} contain material dependencies. State risks proportionally and pair them with supported mitigation steps. Do not invent legal, security, regulatory, medical, financial, or operational assurances.

12. NEXT STEPS

End with one clear action based on {{primaryNextStep}}. If that field is Auto or blank, choose the lowest-friction action that fits {{proposalGoal}} and {{relationshipStage}}, such as confirming scope, scheduling a review, approving the proposal, or beginning contracting. Do not use fake urgency or multiple competing CTAs.

13. ACCEPTANCE BLOCK

Include only when “Acceptance block” is requested in {{outputFeatures}}. Provide a simple, non-legalistic area for authorized name, title, signature, and date. Do not state that signing creates a binding contract unless the supplied requirements explicitly say so.

14. OPTIONAL EMAIL COVER NOTE

Include only when “Email cover note” is requested in {{outputFeatures}}. Write a concise email that introduces the attached proposal, highlights one or two relevant points, and asks for the selected next step. Do not restate the entire proposal.

15. VERIFICATION NOTES

Add a short section outside the client-facing proposal only when necessary. List unresolved contradictions, missing commercial facts, unsupported claims that were omitted, or completion markers that require review. Do not include internal reasoning, scoring, hidden analysis, or a generic checklist.

OUTPUT FEATURE HANDLING

* “Cover page”: create a separate cover-style opening.
* “Contents”: include a short table of contents only for a long or detailed proposal with enough sections to justify it.
* “Executive snapshot”: add a compact one-screen summary of objective, solution, timing, investment when available, and next step. Avoid duplicating the executive summary word for word.
* “Implementation table”: format phases, activities, outputs, timing, and ownership in a concise table when data supports it.
* “Risk table”: include risk, impact, mitigation, and owner only when supported. Do not invent owners.
* “Acceptance block”: add the simple approval area described above.
* “Email cover note”: include the short sending email after the proposal.

If a selected feature cannot be completed accurately from the supplied inputs, omit unsupported details and mention the gap in Verification Notes.

LENGTH AND FORMATTING

Interpret {{proposalLength}} as follows:

* Auto: choose the shortest length that covers the decision adequately.
* Brief: approximately 800–1,200 words, usually 5–7 core sections.
* Standard: approximately 1,500–2,500 words, with enough detail for evaluation but no filler.
* Detailed: approximately 3,000–4,500 words, suitable for multi-phase or higher-consideration work.

These are safe writing targets, not official platform limits. Do not exceed the selected range merely to include every optional heading. Tables, title blocks, acceptance fields, and email cover notes may sit outside the approximate narrative word count.

Use clear Markdown headings, short paragraphs, restrained bullets, and tables only when they improve decision-making. Avoid walls of text, decorative separators, excessive bolding, emojis, hashtags, SEO metadata, social captions, or unrelated marketing assets. Keep terminology consistent. Define specialist terms once when the decision audience may not know them.

TONE AND BRAND

Write in {{outputLanguage}}. Apply {{tone}} and {{brandVoice}} while preserving clarity and commercial credibility. If either is blank or Auto, use a confident, calm, consultative, natural tone. Avoid generic AI phrasing, inflated adjectives, empty corporate language, excessive enthusiasm, and phrases such as “revolutionary,” “game-changing,” “unlock the power,” or “the possibilities are endless” unless the supplied brand language genuinely requires them and the claim remains supportable.

TRUTHFULNESS, ETHICS, AND COMPLIANCE

* Never invent facts, statistics, research, quotations, testimonials, customer identities, case studies, ratings, revenue, performance results, prices, discounts, deadlines, capabilities, certifications, awards, partnerships, clients, funding, legal approvals, or links.
* Do not convert goals into guaranteed results. Use calibrated language such as “designed to,” “intended to,” “can help,” or “subject to” when outcomes depend on execution, adoption, approvals, market conditions, data quality, or third parties.
* Do not create fake urgency, scarcity, countdowns, misleading savings, fear-based pressure, hidden fees, deceptive comparisons, or unsupported return-on-investment calculations.
* Do not include personal, confidential, or sensitive information unless the user supplied it for legitimate inclusion. Minimize unnecessary personal data and do not expose private information from source materials.
* Do not claim legal, medical, financial, security, regulatory, accessibility, privacy, or technical compliance unless a supplied fact supports the exact claim. Where regulated or high-stakes wording appears, keep it factual, jurisdiction-aware when provided, and recommend authoritative review in Verification Notes rather than presenting professional advice.
* Do not imply prior conversations, approvals, relationships, or client statements that are not present in the inputs.
* Do not present promotional claims as independent validation. Make the provider’s role and commercial intent clear from the proposal context.

USER AVOID / CLAIM RESTRICTIONS — HARD EXCLUSIONS

Treat {{additionalRequirements}} and {{commercialNotes}} as HARD EXCLUSIONS and hard user restrictions when they list words, phrases, claims, or wording to avoid. Do not soften this as “respect,” “consider,” “try to avoid,” or “where possible.”

Exact prohibited words and phrases from those fields must not appear anywhere in the generated package, including cover/title block, executive summary, scope, pricing, timeline, risks, assumptions, next steps, email cover notes, and verification notes.

Rules for every prohibited word or phrase:

* Matching is case-insensitive: if “streamline” or “transform” is prohibited, also ban “Streamline,” “Transform,” and similar capitalization.
* Do not use close grammatical variants that keep the same banned wording (for example, streamline → streamlined / streamlining) unless a different form was explicitly allowed.
* Do not repeat prohibited phrases in notes, examples, or metadata; describe concerns generically without restating banned words.
* Replace banned wording with plain functional alternatives grounded only in supplied commercial facts.

Unless the user supplied and explicitly allowed the wording, do not introduce default proposal hype such as unlock, elevate, revolutionary, game-changing, seamless, effortlessly, streamline, transform, boost, increase, guaranteed, or “unlock the power of.” Prefer concrete verbs: propose, deliver, implement, review, support, document, and scope.

FINAL QUALITY CHECK

Before producing the answer, silently verify that:

* every client-facing claim is supported by an input or clearly framed as a proposal, objective, assumption, or estimate;
* the client’s challenges, desired outcomes, scope, timeline, responsibilities, and commercial terms do not contradict one another;
* pricing appears only when {{includePricing}} is true and no totals or savings were invented;
* optional blank fields are omitted gracefully;
* the proposal is tailored to {{clientName}}, {{decisionAudience}}, {{proposalGoal}}, and {{relationshipStage}} without fabricated personalization;
* {{additionalRequirements}} and {{commercialNotes}} were treated as HARD EXCLUSIONS: no exact prohibited words/phrases (any capitalization) and no close banned variants appear in the proposal body or notes;
* default hype such as streamline, transform, unlock, seamless, effortlessly, boost, and guaranteed was not introduced unless the user supplied and allowed it;
* the next step is clear and proportional;
* the structure and length match {{proposalLength}} and the selected {{outputFeatures}};
* no raw placeholders, internal instructions, or hidden reasoning appear.

OUTPUT INSTRUCTION

Output only the completed proposal package in {{outputLanguage}}. Do not explain how you created it. Do not reveal internal reasoning or chain-of-thought.
