GOAL

Create a podcast episode that sounds natural when spoken aloud and provides clear value throughout the target duration.

The episode should:

- deliver the promised topic or outcome;
- maintain a clear narrative or conversational direction;
- sound like a real host rather than a written article read aloud;
- use natural spoken transitions;
- distinguish facts, opinions, estimates, and personal experiences;
- avoid fabricated conversations and quotations;
- include enough context for listeners who cannot see the host or supporting visuals;
- respect the requested format and duration;
- leave the listener with one clear takeaway or next step.

Prioritize:

- spoken clarity;
- factual accuracy;
- listener retention;
- coherent structure;
- natural pacing;
- useful substance;
- transparent commercial messaging;
- realistic production requirements.

DURATION AND WORD COUNT

Estimate script length using a natural speaking pace.

Use these approximate ranges:

- reflective or emotional narration: 110–135 words per minute;
- normal conversational narration: 130–155 words per minute;
- energetic educational narration: 145–170 words per minute;
- interview or co-host conversation: allow additional time for pauses, reactions, and natural variation.

Do not treat the word count as exact.

Account for:

- intro and outro music;
- pauses;
- guest responses;
- sponsor segments;
- sound clips;
- transitions;
- listener exercises;
- moments of reflection.

For a fully scripted solo episode, estimate the target word range.

For an interview, roundtable, or unscripted conversation, do not write enough host copy to fill the entire duration. Create a realistic run-of-show with question timing and expected discussion space.

Do not pad the episode with repetitive explanations merely to reach {{duration}} minutes.

FORMAT-SPECIFIC RULES

SOLO EPISODE

For a solo episode:

- write complete spoken narration;
- keep the host’s argument or story moving forward;
- use natural verbal transitions;
- vary sentence length;
- include brief recaps only when the episode is complex;
- avoid sounding like an SEO article;
- avoid reading long lists without explanation;
- include production cues only when useful.

A recommended solo structure may include:

1. Cold open
2. Brief podcast identification
3. Listener promise
4. Context
5. Main sections
6. Example or case
7. Limitation or counterpoint
8. Practical takeaway
9. CTA
10. Outro

Do not force every section when the topic requires a simpler structure.

INTERVIEW

For an interview:

- create a host introduction;
- explain why the guest is relevant;
- create a logical question sequence;
- include optional follow-up prompts;
- include transitions between topics;
- prepare a concise closing;
- leave realistic time for answers.

Do not invent the guest’s answers.

When {{guestAnswers}} contains real approved answers:

- integrate them accurately;
- preserve the guest’s meaning;
- do not strengthen or dramatize the quotation;
- identify edited or shortened answers where relevant.

When guest answers were not supplied, output:

HOST:
[Question]

FOLLOW-UP OPTIONS:
- [Follow-up]
- [Follow-up]

GUEST:
[Guest response — not scripted]

Do not write fictional guest dialogue.

Do not make both participants sound identical.

CO-HOST CONVERSATION

For a co-host episode:

- assign each host a clear function or perspective;
- create natural handoffs;
- avoid perfectly alternating one sentence at a time;
- avoid constant agreement;
- include respectful clarification or contrast where supported;
- make the conversation advance rather than circle around one point.

Do not invent strong personal opinions for either host unless supplied.

Do not create fake spontaneous reactions such as laughter, surprise, or disagreement without a clear production reason.

ROUND TABLE

For a roundtable:

- introduce every participant concisely;
- establish the central question;
- divide the conversation into clear themes;
- give each participant space;
- include moderator prompts;
- prevent several speakers from repeating the same point;
- finish with a concise final contribution from each participant when appropriate.

Do not invent participant answers.

NARRATIVE OR DOCUMENTARY EPISODE

For narrative or documentary audio:

- establish the central tension early;
- introduce people, time, and place clearly;
- distinguish narration from archival material;
- use scenes only when supported;
- provide context without stopping the story for long lectures;
- use sound cues selectively;
- identify uncertainty;
- avoid fictional reconstruction unless explicitly requested and clearly labelled.

Do not invent:

- dialogue;
- private thoughts;
- weather;
- room details;
- gestures;
- chronology;
- archival audio;
- witness testimony.

When dramatized reconstruction is allowed, label it:

[DRAMATIZED RECONSTRUCTION BASED ON: supplied evidence]

NEWS OR CURRENT-AFFAIRS EPISODE

For news analysis:

- state what happened and when;
- distinguish confirmed reporting from analysis;
- explain why the development matters;
- identify what remains unknown;
- avoid sensational language;
- use approved sources;
- avoid presenting speculation as fact.

Do not invent motives, anonymous sources, quotations, casualties, forecasts, or official responses.

GUIDED EXERCISE

For meditation, reflection, breathing, journaling, or another guided exercise:

- explain the purpose;
- state relevant safety limitations;
- use calm and clear instructions;
- include realistic pauses;
- avoid rushing;
- avoid guaranteed emotional or medical outcomes;
- provide an easy way to stop or modify the exercise.

Represent pauses with timing cues such as:

[PAUSE: 5 seconds]

Do not fill pauses with unnecessary narration.

VIDEO PODCAST

For a video podcast:

- ensure the audio remains understandable without the visual;
- add visual cues only when they provide useful context;
- do not say “as you can see” without describing what is shown;
- identify possible short visual demonstrations;
- mark moments suitable for clips.

Use:

[ON SCREEN: description]

only when a visual element is actually available or planned.

COLD OPEN

Write a cold open when it suits the format.

The cold open should:

- establish the central question, tension, scene, or value;
- create interest without misleading the listener;
- remain concise;
- lead naturally into the episode;
- avoid giving a long podcast introduction before the topic.

Possible cold-open approaches:

- direct conclusion;
- short story moment;
- verified quotation;
- difficult question;
- meaningful contrast;
- concise listener problem;
- brief preview clip from an approved interview.

Do not use empty hooks such as:

- “You won’t believe what happened.”
- “This changes everything.”
- “Nobody is talking about this.”
- “Stay until the end.”
- “The truth will shock you.”
- “This is the episode you’ve been waiting for.”

Do not create a fake preview quote from a guest.

INTRODUCTION

After the cold open, include a concise introduction appropriate to the podcast.

The introduction may include:

- podcast name;
- host name;
- episode topic;
- listener promise;
- necessary content note;
- brief sponsor identification when required.

Do not spend several minutes introducing the podcast unless the format requires it.

Avoid:

- long biographies;
- repeated podcast slogans;
- extensive subscription requests before delivering value;
- generic greetings;
- explaining the full episode structure unless that helps comprehension.

MAIN BODY

Organize the main body into logical segments.

Every segment should:

- have a clear function;
- introduce new information;
- connect to the main message;
- include evidence, explanation, example, or discussion;
- transition naturally to the next segment.

Do not:

- repeat the episode promise in every section;
- restate the same argument using different words;
- introduce several unrelated subtopics;
- include facts without explaining their relevance;
- use long lists that are difficult to follow in audio;
- depend on charts or visual information without verbal explanation.

For complex information:

- group points into small sets;
- explain numbers in listener-friendly terms;
- repeat only the most important figure;
- compare unfamiliar quantities with useful context;
- avoid reading long URLs or citations aloud.

SPOKEN-LANGUAGE STYLE

Write for the ear, not for the page.

Use:

- natural sentence rhythm;
- contractions where appropriate;
- clear pronouns;
- conversational transitions;
- occasional repetition for comprehension;
- concrete language;
- moderate sentence length;
- pronunciation-friendly wording.

Avoid:

- paragraphs that require visual scanning;
- overly complex nested sentences;
- dense strings of statistics;
- unexplained abbreviations;
- excessive parenthetical remarks;
- academic footnote language in narration;
- keyword stuffing;
- artificial banter;
- corporate slogans.

Read every sentence mentally as spoken dialogue.

Rewrite sentences that would sound unnatural aloud.

TRANSITIONS

Use transitions that reflect the actual logic of the episode.

Useful transitions may:

- connect cause and consequence;
- move from context to example;
- introduce a complication;
- compare two interpretations;
- return to the central question;
- move from explanation to action.

Avoid repetitive transitions such as:

- “Now let’s dive deeper.”
- “But wait, there’s more.”
- “Moving on to the next point.”
- “Here’s where things get interesting.”
- “Without further ado.”
- “At the end of the day.”

Do not announce every transition explicitly.

STORIES AND EXAMPLES

Use only approved stories and examples.

When including a story:

- establish who, when, and where only from supplied facts;
- explain why it matters;
- avoid unnecessary dramatization;
- preserve uncertainty;
- do not invent dialogue or internal thoughts;
- do not present one anecdote as general proof.

When an example is hypothetical, label it clearly:

“Imagine a hypothetical situation…”

Do not make the hypothetical example resemble a real customer testimonial.

STATISTICS AND NUMBERS

Use statistics only when supplied and relevant.

When reading numbers aloud:

- simplify presentation without changing meaning;
- avoid unnecessary decimal precision;
- provide context;
- explain the source when important;
- do not overwhelm the listener with several figures in one sentence.

Write pronunciations naturally.

“47.83%”

Prefer:

when exact precision is unnecessary.

Do not alter legally, scientifically, or financially important values.

INTERVIEW QUESTIONS

For interview episodes, organize questions into a progression.

Recommended order:

1. Short introductory question.
2. Relevant background.
3. Central problem or experience.
4. Detailed explanation.
5. Example or evidence.
6. Challenge, limitation, or disagreement.
7. Practical implications.
8. Future direction.
9. Final takeaway.

Questions should:

- be open enough to produce meaningful answers;
- ask one main thing at a time;
- avoid embedding an unsupported assumption;
- avoid telling the guest what answer is expected;
- avoid excessive praise;
- avoid long host monologues before the question;
- include optional follow-ups.

Do not ask:

- questions that require confidential information;
- accusations presented as facts;
- legal or medical conclusions without adequate basis;
- several unrelated questions in one sentence.

Also identify:

- essential questions;
- optional questions;
- questions to cut if time runs short;
- sensitive questions requiring guest approval.
