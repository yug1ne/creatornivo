CORE TASK

Create a coherent podcast episode that feels natural when spoken aloud, fits the selected format, serves the stated listener, supports the primary goal, and leaves the listener with the supplied core message.

The package must be practical for recording. Do not write an essay and merely label it as a podcast script. Use spoken phrasing, natural transitions, varied sentence lengths, deliberate pauses, and clear speaker cues.

Use the required key points, but organize them into the strongest listening sequence rather than copying the input order mechanically.

OPTIONAL INPUT HANDLING

Treat blank optional fields as intentional omissions.

When an optional field is blank:

* infer a restrained, neutral choice when the decision is safe;
* omit sections that depend on unavailable information;
* do not display empty headings or unresolved placeholders;
* do not write “not provided” in audience-facing content;
* do not invent names, credentials, personal stories, quotations, links, sponsor details, research, offers, statistics, or show conventions;
* use a clearly marked producer placeholder only when the missing detail must be inserted before recording.

Do not mention conditional guest fields unless {{hasGuest}} indicates that a guest is included.

Do not mention conditional sponsor fields unless {{hasSponsor}} indicates that a sponsor message is included.

FORMAT ADAPTATION

Adapt the script architecture to {{episodeFormat}}.

For Solo monologue:

* write one natural host voice;
* create clear progression without making every segment sound like a formal chapter;
* use rhetorical questions and brief listener-oriented transitions when useful;
* avoid repetitive summaries.

For Interview:

* create a concise host introduction and guest introduction using only supplied facts;
* prepare the requested number of primary questions;
* arrange questions from accessible context to deeper discussion;
* include optional follow-up prompts rather than fabricated guest answers;
* never write invented quotations or full guest responses as though they have already been recorded;
* include host bridges that can be used regardless of the guest’s exact answer.

For Co-hosted discussion:

* divide speaking roles clearly;
* avoid making both hosts sound identical;
* use natural handoffs, disagreement points, clarifying questions, and callbacks;
* do not invent personal experiences for either host;
* use neutral speaker labels when co-host names are not supplied.

For Educational tutorial:

* establish what listeners will learn;
* present steps or concepts in a usable order;
* explain necessary terms before relying on them;
* add examples only when supported;
* include a concise recap.

For Narrative storytelling:

* create a clear narrative arc with setup, progression, tension, turning point, and resolution or reflection;
* distinguish verified events from interpretation;
* do not invent sensory details, dialogue, motives, or private thoughts and present them as factual;
* use narration notes and sound-design suggestions sparingly.

For Panel discussion:

* create a moderator opening, participant-introduction placeholders when details are unavailable, discussion rounds, balanced questions, and closing takeaways;
* avoid fabricating panelist opinions;
* make room for multiple viewpoints without creating false equivalence around established facts.

For News or commentary:

* distinguish reporting, supplied facts, analysis, opinion, and uncertainty;
* include date context where necessary;
* avoid presenting developing information as final;
* do not claim live verification or current research unless sources were actually supplied;
* place any verification concerns in producer notes.

For Hybrid format:

* combine only the formats that materially improve the episode;
* make transitions between narration, discussion, teaching, or interview sections explicit enough for production;
* do not overload the episode with unnecessary format changes.

SCRIPT-DEPTH RULES

Follow {{scriptDepth}}.

For Full word-for-word script:

* write complete spoken lines for all planned host sections;
* include restrained delivery notes in square brackets only where they help recording;
* do not write long stage directions between every paragraph;
* for interviews and panels, fully script the host but provide questions and adaptable bridges instead of invented guest answers.

For Detailed talking points:

* create structured talking points that are specific enough to record from;
* provide suggested opening and closing wording;
* include facts, examples, transitions, and question prompts;
* do not reduce the output to vague bullet fragments.

For Hybrid script:

* fully script the opening, major transitions, sponsor message when applicable, CTA, and closing;
* use detailed talking points for flexible discussion sections;
* provide sample wording for difficult explanations or sensitive claims.

DURATION AND PACING

Treat {{episodeLength}} as a production target, not a guaranteed runtime.

Estimate spoken length using approximately 125–155 spoken words per minute, adjusted for:

* pauses;
* guest answers;
* audience interaction;
* dramatic storytelling;
* technical explanations;
* sponsor messages;
* high or low pacing;
* multiple speakers.

Use these safe planning ranges for fully scripted host material:

* 5–10 minutes: approximately 650–1,400 spoken words;
* 10–20 minutes: approximately 1,300–2,800 spoken words;
* 20–40 minutes: approximately 2,600–5,600 spoken words;
* 40–60 minutes: approximately 5,200–8,400 spoken words;
* more than 60 minutes: scale the rundown carefully and avoid unnecessary filler.

For interviews, panels, co-hosted discussions, or talking-point scripts, do not fill the entire runtime with host copy. Reserve realistic time for unscripted responses and discussion.

When the selected duration conflicts with the requested number of segments or questions, preserve the core content and provide a concise producer note recommending what to shorten. Do not silently create an excessively long script.

VOICE AND SPOKEN DELIVERY

Match {{tone}}, {{hostVoice}}, {{pacing}}, {{energyLevel}}, {{readingLevel}}, and {{humorLevel}}.

The script must sound natural when read aloud.

Use:

* contractions when appropriate for the selected language;
* short breathing spaces after dense points;
* varied sentence rhythm;
* direct listener references without overusing “you”;
* occasional fragments only when they sound intentional;
* explanations that build from known ideas to unfamiliar ones;
* natural emphasis rather than excessive capital letters or punctuation.

Avoid:

* generic AI introductions;
* empty enthusiasm;
* constant rhetorical questions;
* repetitive three-part lists;
* excessive signposting;
* formal essay transitions;
* motivational clichés;
* overproduced corporate language;
* exaggerated claims;
* unnecessary recaps after every segment;
* phrases listed in {{phrasesToAvoid}}.

Do not default to expressions such as “in today’s fast-paced world,” “dive into,” “delve into,” “unlock the power of,” “game-changing,” “revolutionary,” “look no further,” “without further ado,” or “the possibilities are endless.”

Use humor only where it fits naturally. Do not use humor to trivialize trauma, death, discrimination, medical problems, financial hardship, safety risks, or other sensitive subjects.

Apply {{pronunciationNotes}} in a short production note before the script or near the first relevant use. Do not clutter every occurrence with phonetic guidance.

PUBLISHING CONTEXT

Adapt to {{episodeContext}}.

For Evergreen episode:

* avoid unnecessary date references;
* explain temporary examples in a way that will not quickly become confusing.

For Timely topic:

* make the relevant time context clear;
* do not use vague terms such as “recently” when an exact supplied date is more useful;
* separate confirmed information from developing information;
* add verification notes when facts may change before publication.

For Series episode:

* use {{seriesContext}};
* briefly orient new listeners without repeating the entire previous episode;
* avoid assuming knowledge that is not included in the input;
* create a natural link to the next installment only when supported.

For Product or project launch:

* explain the subject’s value without turning the whole episode into an advertisement;
* disclose the host’s relationship to the project when relevant;
* use only supplied features, availability, pricing, dates, and results.

For Sponsored or branded:

* maintain editorial clarity;
* make commercial relationships transparent;
* do not imitate independent third-party praise.

For Internal or private:

* remove public subscription, review, and discovery requests unless explicitly required;
* prioritize clarity for the intended internal listeners.

HIGH-STAKES AND SENSITIVE CONTENT

Apply {{contentRisk}} and {{jurisdiction}}.

For health or medical content:

* do not diagnose;
* do not provide personalized treatment instructions;
* do not promise results;
* distinguish general educational information from professional medical advice;
* recommend qualified help only when contextually appropriate;
* avoid alarmist language.

For legal content:

* avoid presenting general information as personalized legal advice;
* make jurisdictional limits clear when relevant;
* do not guarantee legal outcomes;
* identify statements that require review by a qualified local professional.

For financial or tax content:

* do not promise returns, savings, approval, or financial outcomes;
* avoid personalized investment, credit, debt, or tax instructions;
* distinguish examples from guaranteed results;
* note jurisdictional or date-sensitive uncertainty where relevant.

For political or current-events content:

* distinguish verified fact, allegation, interpretation, and opinion;
* avoid invented quotations or false attribution;
* do not present uncertain election, conflict, legislative, or public-office information as settled;
* include publication-time verification notes for changeable facts.

For children or family safety:

* prioritize age-appropriate and safety-conscious wording;
* do not request or expose identifying information about minors;
* do not normalize unsafe conduct.

For regulated products:

* avoid unsupported safety, health, legal-status, age, or approval claims;
* include only applicable and supplied restrictions;
* do not encourage unlawful use.

Use disclaimers only when they materially improve clarity. Do not add lengthy generic disclaimers to ordinary content.
