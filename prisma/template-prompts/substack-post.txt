GOAL

Write a Substack post that gives readers a clear reason to open, continue reading, and remain subscribed.

The post should:

- communicate one central argument, story, question, or lesson;
- provide enough substance to justify the reader’s time;
- sound like a recognizable author rather than a generic content brand;
- distinguish facts, interpretation, opinion, and personal experience;
- reward existing subscribers while remaining understandable to new readers;
- use a paywall only when the paid section contains meaningful additional value;
- support the publication’s long-term relationship with readers;
- avoid artificial cliffhangers, exaggerated claims, and generic newsletter language.

POST TYPE

Adapt the structure to {{postType}}.

Possible post types include:

- personal essay;
- opinion column;
- reported analysis;
- educational article;
- how-to guide;
- industry commentary;
- news analysis;
- research summary;
- interview;
- Q&A;
- case study;
- product or project update;
- behind-the-scenes post;
- book or media review;
- curated digest;
- subscriber letter;
- paid deep dive;
- publication announcement;
- open thread or discussion post.

Do not use the same structure for every post type.

PERSONAL ESSAY

For a personal essay:

- begin with a specific moment, observation, tension, or question;
- use only approved personal experience;
- connect the personal story to a broader idea;
- avoid presenting one experience as universal evidence;
- include reflection rather than only chronology;
- end with a meaningful realization, unresolved question, or practical conclusion.

Do not invent dialogue, memories, emotions, locations, or events.

Do not exaggerate vulnerability for engagement.

OPINION OR COMMENTARY

For an opinion post:

- state the central position clearly;
- explain the reasoning;
- support factual claims with supplied evidence;
- distinguish opinion from confirmed fact;
- acknowledge the strongest relevant counterargument;
- explain where the author remains uncertain;
- avoid false balance when the evidence is not balanced;
- avoid attacking readers who disagree.

Do not manufacture controversy merely to attract comments.

ANALYSIS OR RESEARCH POST

For an analytical post:

- define the central question;
- explain the relevant context;
- identify the strongest available evidence;
- separate evidence from interpretation;
- address uncertainty and competing explanations;
- explain why the conclusion matters;
- provide sources or notes when supplied.

Do not invent datasets, research papers, experts, surveys, or consensus.

EDUCATIONAL OR HOW-TO POST

For an educational post:

- answer the main reader need early;
- explain prerequisite knowledge;
- organize the information logically;
- use steps only when the process is sequential;
- include examples only when supplied;
- explain common mistakes;
- mention limitations, exceptions, and decision criteria;
- give the reader a practical next step.

Do not pad a simple topic into a long guide.

NEWS ANALYSIS

For a news or current-events post:

- identify the confirmed development;
- include the relevant date or period;
- distinguish reporting from commentary;
- explain what changed;
- provide necessary context;
- state what remains unknown;
- avoid sensational language;
- cite supplied sources.

Do not present an unverified report as settled fact.

Do not invent motives, quotations, official reactions, casualties, or consequences.

INTERVIEW OR Q&A

For an interview post:

- use only supplied questions and approved responses;
- do not invent answers or quotations;
- lightly edit for clarity only when permitted;
- preserve the interviewee’s intended meaning;
- provide concise context before the interview;
- highlight useful themes without distorting statements.

Clearly mark edited, condensed, or translated responses when relevant.

DIGEST OR CURATED POST

For a digest:

- include only supplied items;
- organize them by importance, theme, or chronology;
- explain why each item matters;
- provide enough context for the reader to evaluate it;
- avoid copying full article text;
- do not turn the post into a list of unexplained links;
- disclose affiliate or commercial relationships.

PRODUCT OR PROJECT UPDATE

For a product or project update:

- explain what changed;
- identify who the change affects;
- describe the practical benefit;
- distinguish released, beta, planned, and experimental features;
- mention relevant limitations;
- include one clear next step.

Do not invent release dates, features, integrations, pricing, adoption, or customer results.

2. Do not invent:

- conversations;

4. Do not use phrases such as:

- “People keep asking me…”
- “My readers often tell me…”

5. Clearly distinguish:

7. Do not make medical, legal, financial, mental-health, safety, career, or business guarantees.

9. When information is incomplete, state the uncertainty rather than filling the gap.

TITLE

Write one recommended post title and four alternate options.

The recommended title should:

- accurately represent the post;
- identify the central subject or tension;
- create interest through specificity;
- sound natural in the author’s voice;
- remain understandable outside the publication;
- avoid misleading curiosity;
- avoid unnecessary punctuation;
- avoid excessive length.

Create alternate titles using different approaches:

1. Direct and descriptive.
2. Argument- or conclusion-led.
3. Question- or tension-led.
4. Personal or narrative-led, only when supported.
5. Search- or topic-led, when discoverability matters.

Do not create five titles that differ only through words such as:

- why;
- how;
- what;
- truth;
- secret.

Avoid title patterns such as:

- “You Won’t Believe…”
- “Nobody Is Talking About This”
- “This Changes Everything”
- “The Truth About…”
- “The Secret They Don’t Want You to Know”
- “My Life Changed When…”
- “The Ultimate Guide”
- “Everything You Need to Know”

unless the wording accurately reflects the content and is not exaggerated.

OPENING

Begin with a strong, relevant opening.

The opening may use:

- a specific scene;
- a concrete observation;
- a surprising but verified fact;
- an unresolved question;
- a clear argument;
- a contradiction;
- a concise update;
- a direct statement of what changed.

- “Hello everyone.”
- “Welcome back.”
- “I hope this email finds you well.”
- “In today’s fast-paced world…”
- “This week, I wanted to talk about…”
- “Let’s dive in.”
- a long explanation of why the author has not published recently, unless that is the actual topic.

Do not withhold all useful information solely to create a cliffhanger.

Headings should communicate meaning.

- Background
- Main Section
- Discussion

Do not use artificial transitions such as:

- “But wait, there’s more.”
- “Here’s where it gets interesting.”
- “Now let’s dive deeper.”
- “This brings us to the next point.”

DEPTH AND VALUE

FREE AND PAID CONTENT

Follow {{accessType}} and {{paywallStrategy}}.

For a free post:

- provide the complete promised value;
- do not artificially withhold the conclusion;
- use a subscription CTA only when natural.

For a paid-subscriber-only post:

- make the title and public preview accurately describe the content;
- do not reveal the entire paid analysis in the preview;
- ensure the full post provides substantial value beyond the teaser.

For a free preview with paid continuation:

- give free readers a useful and complete opening section;
- establish the question, context, or initial insight;
- place the paywall at a natural transition;
- explain what paid readers will receive;
- ensure the paid section contains meaningful analysis, evidence, examples, instructions, or conclusions.

Do not place the paywall:

- in the middle of a sentence;
- before the reader understands the topic;
- immediately after a clickbait opening;
- after giving away the entire paid conclusion;
- solely to frustrate free readers.

Use this marker:

[PAYWALL]

Before the marker, add a short bridge such as:

Do not write a generic phrase such as:

- place the disclosure before or near the commercial recommendation;

- do not disguise advertising as editorial analysis;
- do not invent personal use or endorsement.
