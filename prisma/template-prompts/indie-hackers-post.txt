GOAL

Write an Indie Hackers post that contributes useful information to other founders, makers, developers, marketers, and independent business owners.

The post should help readers understand at least one of the following:

- what the author built;
- why the author made a particular decision;
- what experiment was performed;
- what result occurred;
- what went wrong;
- what the author learned;
- what trade-off was discovered;
- what the author plans to test next;
- what specific feedback is needed.

The post should remain valuable even when the reader does not click the product link.

The post must not be a thin advertisement surrounded by generic founder language.

COMMUNITY-FIRST PRINCIPLE

The post should contribute before promoting.

Before including a product link, make sure the post provides at least one meaningful element:

- a real lesson;
- a transparent result;
- a decision framework;
- a technical explanation;
- an experiment breakdown;
- a useful mistake;
- a concrete process;
- a specific founder question;
- a clear trade-off;
- an honest retrospective.

Do not use Indie Hackers only as a source of traffic or backlinks.

Do not publish a one-paragraph link drop.

Do not pretend to seek feedback when the real purpose is only promotion.

PLATFORM AND COMMUNITY FIT

Write for a community of builders and founders.

Assume readers may ask detailed questions about:

- pricing;
- revenue;
- acquisition;
- conversion;
- technology;
- product-market fit;
- customer interviews;
- validation;
- costs;
- margins;
- competition;
- roadmap;
- founder involvement;
- lessons learned.

Do not avoid relevant detail merely to protect a polished brand image.

At the same time, do not reveal confidential, personally identifiable, contractually restricted, or security-sensitive information.

TRANSPARENCY

Clearly disclose the author’s relationship to the product.

Use natural wording such as:

- “I’m building…”
- “This is my product…”
- “My co-founder and I launched…”
- “I work on the team behind…”
- “I’m sharing the results from…”

only when supported by {{authorRelationship}}.

Do not write as though the author is:

- an independent customer;
- an unbiased reviewer;
- a journalist;
- an unrelated community member;
- a satisfied user;

when the author owns, builds, markets, or profits from the product.

Do not create a fake success story from the perspective of a customer.

ACCURACY

1. Use only supplied facts, metrics, dates, timelines, quotations, product details, experiments, and personal experiences.

2. Do not invent:

- MRR;
- ARR;
- revenue;
- profit;
- expenses;
- customers;
- users;
- traffic;
- conversion rates;
- churn;
- retention;

- support messages;
- outreach replies;
- partnerships;
- investors;
- press coverage;
- acquisition offers;
- product features;
- integrations;
- experiments;
- technical benchmarks;
- personal experiences.

3. Do not imply that the author personally completed, tested, or observed something unless the information was supplied.

4. Do not use phrases such as:

- “Users kept asking for…”
- “People loved it…”
- “Customers were excited…”
- “Everyone told me…”
- “I talked to dozens of founders…”
- “The launch went viral…”
- “We grew organically…”
- “I made my first $1,000…”

unless supported by approved evidence.

5. Clearly distinguish:

- confirmed data;
- estimate;
- assumption;
- hypothesis;
- anecdote;
- author interpretation;
- planned action;
- unresolved question.

6. Do not convert correlation into causation.

7. Do not describe one customer conversation as broad market validation.

8. Do not describe signups as active users.

9. Do not describe total revenue as recurring revenue.

10. Do not describe revenue as profit.

11. Do not describe a short-term increase as sustainable growth.

12. Do not describe visitors, waitlist signups, free accounts, trials, paid customers, and active customers as interchangeable.

13. When required information is missing, use:

Do not fill the gap with plausible-sounding startup data.

PRODUCT-STAGE ACCURACY

Represent the product’s current stage honestly.

For an idea-stage project:

- do not describe it as a startup with customers;
- focus on the problem, hypothesis, and validation plan;
- ask for problem-level feedback rather than praise for the solution.

For a prototype:

- distinguish clickable mockups from working functionality;
- explain what has and has not been implemented;
- avoid claiming product-market validation.

For an MVP:

- explain the narrow current scope;
- disclose major limitations;
- distinguish early usage from stable adoption.

For beta or Early Access:

- explain access conditions;
- mention important limitations;
- distinguish beta features from production-ready functionality.

For a launched product:

- identify the actual availability;
- state pricing accurately;
- do not imply broad adoption without evidence.

For a paused, pivoting, or shutting-down project:

- explain the decision honestly;
- avoid blaming users;
- distinguish business failure, strategic change, personal constraints, and technical limitations.

TITLE

Write one recommended title and four alternate titles.

The title should:

- make the subject clear;
- identify the actual result, decision, problem, experiment, or question;
- contain concrete information when supplied;
- avoid promotional slogans;
- avoid misleading curiosity;
- sound natural in a founder community;
- remain concise enough to scan.

Use five meaningfully different approaches:

1. Direct result or update.
2. Lesson-led.
3. Experiment-led.
4. Problem- or mistake-led.
5. Question-led.

Examples of useful title structures:

- “What I learned after [confirmed action]”
- “I tested [confirmed experiment]: here are the results”
- “Why I changed [confirmed product decision]”
- “From [confirmed starting point] to [confirmed result] in [period]”
- “How would you solve [specific founder problem]?”
- “I shut down [project]: the numbers and mistakes”
- “[Metric] after [period]: what worked and what didn’t”

Do not use a number unless it was supplied and clearly defined.

Avoid titles such as:

- “My startup journey”
- “Big news!”
- “We finally launched!”
- “This changes everything”
- “The next big thing”
- “How I built a six-figure business” unless verified;
- “From zero to hero”
- “My product went viral”
- “The secret to startup success”
- “I built an app that will revolutionize…”
- “Please support my launch”

OPENING

Begin with the most relevant fact, decision, result, failure, or question.

Useful opening approaches include:

- the confirmed result;
- the unexpected outcome;
- the specific founder problem;
- the decision being explained;
- the experiment and its result;
- the point at which the original plan failed;
- the exact feedback needed.

The first paragraph should answer:

- What happened?
- Why should another founder care?
- What will the post explain?

Do not begin with:

- “Hey Indie Hackers!”
- “I’m excited to share…”
- “After months of hard work…”
- “Ever since I was a child…”
- “In today’s fast-paced startup world…”
- “I finally took the leap…”
- “This has been an incredible journey…”
- a long biography;
- a vague motivational statement.

PROJECT INTRODUCTION

When introducing a project, explain concisely:

- what it does;
- who it is for;
- what problem it addresses;
- its current stage;
- what is meaningfully different;
- why the author is discussing it now.

Do not include a long list of every feature.

Do not call the product:

- revolutionary;
- unique;
- disruptive;
- groundbreaking;
- all-in-one;
- the future of;
- game-changing;

unless the claim is supported and adds factual meaning.

BUILD-IN-PUBLIC UPDATE

For a build-in-public post, use a structure such as:

1. What changed since the previous update.
2. What was completed.
3. What did not go as planned.
4. Confirmed numbers or signals.
5. What was learned.
6. What will be tested next.
7. One specific community question.

Do not make every small coding task sound like a major milestone.

Do not list completed tasks without explaining their significance.

Do not hide setbacks merely to maintain a successful founder image.

MONTHLY OR WEEKLY UPDATE

For recurring updates, compare the current period with an appropriate earlier period only when comparable data was supplied.

Do not publish vanity metrics without explaining what they indicate.

Do not describe normal metric fluctuation as a trend.

LAUNCH POST

For a launch announcement:

1. State what launched.
2. Explain who it is for.
3. Explain the problem.
4. Describe the current product scope.
5. State pricing or access conditions.
6. Disclose beta or Early Access limitations.
7. Explain what kind of feedback is needed.
8. Include one product link.

Do not make the whole post a launch-page copy block.

Do not ask readers to:

- sign up;
- upvote;
- share;
- follow;
- comment;
- and purchase

all at once.

Do not claim that a launch was successful before results exist.

IDEA-VALIDATION POST

For idea validation:

- focus on the problem rather than selling the proposed solution;
- explain the assumed audience;
- identify existing alternatives;
- state what has and has not been validated;
- ask specific behavioral questions;
- avoid leading questions.

Do not ask:

- “Would you use this?”
- “Would you pay for this?”
- “Is this a good idea?”

as the only validation method.

Prefer questions such as:

- “How do you currently handle [problem]?”
- “What is the hardest part of [process]?”
- “What have you already tried?”
- “What causes you to switch tools?”
- “When did this last happen?”

Do not invent customer research that has not occurred.

CUSTOMER-RESEARCH POST

When reporting customer research:

- state how participants were recruited;
- state the number of conversations only when supplied;
- explain who was interviewed;
- separate direct quotations from interpretation;
- identify recurring themes carefully;
- avoid claiming statistical significance from qualitative interviews;
- explain what changed in the product hypothesis.

Do not invent quotations or combine several customer statements into a fake direct quote.

MILESTONE POST

For a milestone:

- define the milestone;
- include the relevant period;
- provide context;
- explain what contributed to it;
- explain what remains unresolved;
- avoid turning the milestone into self-congratulation.

Possible milestones include:

- first paying customer;
- first recurring revenue;
- user threshold;
- launch;
- profitability;
- completed prototype;
- acquisition;
- successful migration;
- retention improvement.

Do not treat:

- free signups;
- impressions;
- one viral post;
- one high-revenue month;
- cumulative revenue

as proof of a sustainable business without context.

REVENUE AND MRR POSTS

When discussing money, clearly state:

- currency;
- reporting period;
- whether figures are gross or net;
- whether taxes are included;
- whether refunds are deducted;
- whether revenue is recurring or one-time;
- whether founder labor is included in costs;
- whether advertising and infrastructure are included;
- whether the business is profitable.

Distinguish:

- revenue;
- MRR;
- ARR;
- bookings;
- GMV;
- gross profit;
- operating profit;
- cash collected;
- contracted revenue;
- lifetime sales.

Do not describe annualized MRR as money already earned.

Do not omit major expenses when describing profitability.

Do not reveal private customer payment data.

GROWTH-EXPERIMENT POST

For a growth experiment, use this structure:

1. Problem or opportunity.
2. Hypothesis.
3. What was changed.
4. Audience and channel.
5. Test period.
6. Sample size.
7. Primary metric.
8. Result.
9. Confounding factors.
10. Interpretation.
11. Next experiment.

Do not claim causation when:

- several variables changed;
- there was no control;
- sample size was very small;
- seasonality affected traffic;
- the audience changed;
- analytics were incomplete.

Do not hide negative results.

A failed experiment can still produce a useful post when the reasoning and lessons are clear.

MARKETING BREAKDOWN

- amount of effort;
- spend;
- duration;

Do not describe unpaid founder labor as cost-free marketing without noting the time involved.

Do not claim a channel “works” based on one result without qualification.

PRICING EXPERIMENT

For pricing:

- state the previous price;
- state the new price;
- explain what changed;
- identify the segment;
- state the experiment period;
- report conversion and revenue effects;
- mention customer reactions only when supplied;
- explain whether plans or product value changed simultaneously.

Do not conclude that a price is optimal from insufficient data.

Do not invent willingness-to-pay research.

TECHNICAL DEEP DIVE

For technical posts:

- explain the original problem;
- identify constraints;
- describe options considered;
- explain the selected approach;
- show important implementation details;
- describe trade-offs;
- include confirmed performance or cost effects;
- identify remaining limitations.

Do not turn a technical post into a product advertisement.

Do not invent:

- benchmarks;
- infrastructure costs;
- load-test results;
- security properties;
- uptime;
- response times;
- scalability;
- framework limitations.

Do not describe a technology as universally best.

Explain why it was suitable for this project.

CODE SNIPPETS

Use code snippets only when supplied or when the requested task explicitly permits generating example code.

When code is included:

- keep it directly relevant;
- remove secrets and credentials;
- avoid exposing private endpoints;
- explain what the code does;
- mention important limitations;
- distinguish production code from simplified examples.

Do not invent a working implementation and present it as code currently used by the project.

Do not include:

- API keys;
- passwords;
- database credentials;
- private customer data;
- access tokens;
- secret URLs.

STACK POSTS

For a technology-stack post:

- explain the role of each major component;
- state why it was selected;

- mention cost only when supplied;
- distinguish current tools from planned migrations;
- avoid affiliate promotion unless disclosed.

Do not list tools without explaining their relevance.

Do not claim that the stack is ideal for every founder.

MISTAKE OR FAILURE POST

For a failure analysis:

1. State what failed.
2. Explain the original expectation.
3. Describe the decision or action.
4. Present the confirmed result.
5. Identify contributing factors.
6. Separate controllable and uncontrollable factors.
7. Explain what would be done differently.
8. Provide a useful lesson.

Do not create fake vulnerability.

Do not exaggerate minor inconvenience into a dramatic collapse.

Do not blame:

- customers;
- employees;
- contractors;
- platforms;
- competitors

without verified evidence and fair context.

Do not reveal private disputes or identifying customer information.

POSTMORTEM

For a shutdown, failed launch, migration failure, outage, or unsuccessful project:

- explain the original goal;
- show the timeline;
- provide relevant numbers;
- identify assumptions;
- explain warning signs;
- describe the decision point;
- explain consequences;
- state what was learned;
- mention unresolved issues.

Do not rewrite history to make every mistake look like an intentional lesson.

Do not imply that failure automatically creates expertise.

PIVOT POST

For a pivot:

- explain the previous direction;
- state what evidence challenged it;
- describe the new direction;
- explain what remains the same;
- identify what is being discarded;
- state what still needs validation.

Do not describe a change based only on founder boredom as market evidence.

Do not imply the new direction is already validated.

SHUTDOWN POST

For a shutdown:

- state the decision clearly;
- explain the main reasons;
- provide relevant context;
- distinguish product, market, financial, team, and personal factors;
- disclose customer transition information only when approved;
- explain what will happen to user data and access only from supplied policy details;
- avoid dramatic blame;
- avoid turning the shutdown into an immediate promotion for another project.

FEEDBACK REQUEST

A feedback request must be specific.

Explain:

- what is being evaluated;
- the target user;
- the current stage;
- the decision the feedback will influence;
- known constraints;
- what kind of response is useful.

Ask no more than three focused questions.

Examples:

- “Does the homepage explain the product within the first few seconds?”
- “Which part of the pricing structure is unclear?”
- “Would you expect this feature under the current navigation label?”
- “Which of these two onboarding approaches creates less friction?”

Avoid:

- “Any feedback?”
- “What do you think?”
- “Is this a good idea?”
- “Roast my landing page” without evaluation criteria;
- asking for praise disguised as feedback.

Do not argue defensively with critical feedback.

COMMUNITY QUESTION

End with one specific question only when the post genuinely benefits from discussion.

A strong question should:

- connect directly to the post;
- be answerable from the reader’s experience;
- help the author make a decision;
- avoid asking several things at once;
- avoid generic engagement bait.

Do not end every post with:

- “Thoughts?”
- “Anyone else?”
- “What do you think?”
- “Would love your feedback.”
- “Who can relate?”

When no discussion is necessary, end with the next planned action or concise lesson.

LINKS AND SELF-PROMOTION

Use only approved links.

Include no more than one primary product link in the main post unless additional links are necessary for evidence or technical context.

The link should appear after enough context has been provided.

Explain what the reader will find after opening it.

Do not:

- hide the commercial destination;
- use misleading anchor text;
- add affiliate links without disclosure;
- create several CTAs;
- link to an unrelated landing page;
- require the reader to click to understand the lesson.

The post should stand on its own.

COMPETITOR REFERENCES

Mention competitors or alternatives only when directly relevant.

Use factual, neutral descriptions.

Do not:

- use competitor names for traffic;
- make unsupported superiority claims;
- misrepresent competitor pricing or features;
- ridicule competitor users;
- imply endorsement;
- copy competitor positioning.

When competitor details were not supplied or verified, describe the category rather than naming a company.

ROADMAP

Discuss roadmap items only when approved.

Clearly label:

- in development;
- under consideration;
- planned;
- experimental;
- not committed;
- scheduled.

Do not promise a delivery date unless confirmed.

Do not present community suggestions as accepted commitments.

Do not imply that a roadmap item will be built merely because it appears in the post.

FUNDING AND INVESTMENT

State funding status accurately.

Do not imply that:

- bootstrapped means profitable;
- funded means successful;
- revenue means self-sustaining;
- investor interest means a completed investment;
- acquisition interest means a formal offer.

Do not disclose private investment terms without approval.

Do not solicit investment unless the post and jurisdiction permit it and the request explicitly requires it.

HIRING OR COLLABORATION

For co-founder, contractor, employee, or collaborator searches:

- state the project stage;
- define the role;
- explain expected responsibilities;
- state compensation or equity only when supplied;
- clarify time commitment;
- state location or timezone requirements;
- describe the current team;
- explain the selection process.

Do not disguise unpaid work as an opportunity.

Do not invent salary, equity, funding, or revenue.

FORMATTING

Use readable plain text or Markdown-compatible formatting.

Use headings only when the post is long enough to require them.

Use bullets for:

- metrics;
- lessons;
- steps;
- options;
- trade-offs;
- next actions.

Do not:

- make every sentence a bullet;
- use excessive bold text;
- create a dense wall of text;
- write one sentence per paragraph throughout the entire post;
- place the full post inside a code block;
- include HTML unless explicitly requested.

Use a table only when comparing structured data and the output format supports it.

PRIVACY AND CONFIDENTIALITY

- full customer names without approval;
- customer email addresses;
- phone numbers;
- billing details;
- private support conversations;
- confidential contracts;
- private analytics identifiers;
- access credentials;
- employee performance information;
- private investor discussions;
- confidential acquisition terms;
- security vulnerabilities;
- unreleased product details marked confidential.

Anonymize customer examples when appropriate.

Do not create fake anonymized customers.

- the post type matches the requested goal;
- the product stage is represented accurately;
