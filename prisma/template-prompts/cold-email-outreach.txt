CORE REQUIREMENTS


Write one subject line and one complete email.
Keep the email under 120 words, excluding the subject line and signature.
Start with a relevant observation, problem, or business context. Do not use a generic greeting or introductory sentence.
Connect the observation naturally to the recipient’s responsibilities.
Present one clear value proposition focused on the recipient’s likely outcome, not a list of product features.
Explain the offer in concrete, plain language.
Include only one primary benefit.
Use the supplied proof only when it directly supports the claim.
End with one low-friction CTA appropriate to the recipient and offer.
Make the email easy to answer with a short response.
PERSONALIZATION AND ACCURACY
Do not invent facts about the recipient, company, role, tools, revenue, customers, hiring activity, recent events, or business challenges.
Use {{specificObservation}} only when it contains a real, verifiable observation.
If no verified observation is supplied, personalize the email around the recipient’s role, industry, or likely workflow without pretending to know private details.
Do not write phrases such as “I saw that,” “I noticed,” or “I came across” unless the supplied information supports them.
Treat {{painPoint}} as a reasonable hypothesis, not a confirmed fact.
Use language such as “you may be dealing with,” “teams in this position often face,” or “this can become difficult when” when certainty is limited.
Do not invent customer names, case studies, statistics, awards, testimonials, or results.
Do not imply an existing relationship when none exists.
HUMAN WRITING STYLE
Write like a thoughtful professional contacting one specific person, not like an automated sales sequence.
Use natural, conversational business language. Keep sentences clear and varied. Sound confident but not overly familiar, pushy, flattering, or enthusiastic.

EMAIL STRUCTURE
Use this general flow, but do not make it feel formulaic:


Relevant opening:
   A specific observation, role-related responsibility, current situation, or plausible challenge.
Problem connection:
   One sentence showing why the issue may matter to this recipient.
Value proposition:
   Explain what {{yourOffer}} helps achieve and how it differs from a generic alternative.
Proof:
   Add one brief credibility signal if valid proof was supplied.
CTA:
   Ask for one small and specific next step.
CTA RULES
The CTA must be low-pressure and easy to answer.
Choose the CTA based on {{desiredNextStep}}. Examples include:


“Would it be useful if I sent over a two-minute example?”
“Worth comparing this with your current process?”
“Should I send a brief breakdown?”
“Open to seeing how this could work for {{companyName}}?”
“Would a short example be relevant?”
“Is this something you’re currently looking at?”
Do not automatically ask for a 15-minute meeting. A call should be suggested only when it is the most natural next step.
SUBJECT LINE
Write one subject line that:
contains 2–6 words;
sounds like an internal business email;
reflects the actual topic;
avoids clickbait;
avoids false urgency;
avoids the recipient’s name unless it sounds natural;
does not use title case for every word;
does not include “quick question.”
FINAL QUALITY CHECK
Before presenting the email, silently verify that:
every personalized detail was supplied;
no fact or result was invented;
the email focuses on the recipient rather than the sender;
the value proposition is concrete;
the pain point is not presented as a confirmed fact without evidence;
the email contains only one main idea;
the CTA is easy to answer;
the wording does not sound like a mass email;
the body is under 120 words;
there are no generic sales clichés.
