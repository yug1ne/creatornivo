GOAL
Write a coherent YouTube script that delivers one clear promise, maintains attention through meaningful progression, and gives the viewer a satisfying conclusion.
The script should feel written for spoken delivery and visual production, not like an article converted into narration.
The video must provide the value promised by its title and opening.
ACCURACY AND RESEARCH

Use only facts, statistics, studies, quotations, dates, examples, and personal experiences supplied in the prompt or approved sources.
Do not invent studies, experts, statistics, historical events, product results, customer stories, quotations, or personal experiences.
Do not imply that the creator personally witnessed or experienced something unless it appears in {{personalExperience}}.
Do not invent credentials, insider access, interviews, conversations, or experiments.
Clearly distinguish:
   * confirmed fact;
   * interpretation;
   * opinion;
   * estimate;
   * hypothesis;
   * disputed claim.
When reliable sources disagree, represent the disagreement accurately rather than selecting one position without context.
Do not exaggerate uncertainty into certainty.
Do not make guaranteed medical, financial, legal, career, business, safety, or performance claims.
Do not cite a source that was not supplied or verified.
Mark places where an additional source or fact check is required using:
[FACT CHECK REQUIRED: describe what must be verified]
Mark where a citation should appear using:
[SOURCE: source name or supplied reference]
VIDEO FORMAT
Adapt the structure to {{videoFormat}}.
Possible formats include:


educational explainer;
step-by-step tutorial;
documentary-style story;
video essay;
commentary;
product review;
comparison;
case study;
list video;
interview-led video;
personal story;
investigative breakdown;
screen-recorded demonstration.
Do not force every topic into three tips or four sections.
Choose the structure that best serves the topic, audience, and video goal.
DURATION AND WORD COUNT
Estimate a realistic spoken word count for {{duration}} minutes.
Use approximately:
110–130 words per minute for serious, emotional, technical, or documentary content;
130–150 words per minute for normal explanatory delivery;
150–165 words per minute for fast, energetic content.
Adjust for pauses, demonstrations, interviews, graphics, screen recordings, and B-roll.
Do not fill the entire runtime with uninterrupted narration when the visual format requires pauses.
Do not pad the script with repetition merely to reach the target duration.
If the supplied material is insufficient for the requested duration, deepen the explanation through relevant context, examples, implications, objections, or practical applications without inventing facts.
OPENING HOOK
Create an opening hook appropriate to the total video length.
The hook will usually occupy approximately the first 5–8% of the video and should rarely exceed 30 seconds unless the format requires a longer cold open.
The hook should:
establish the topic, tension, question, conflict, result, or useful promise;
identify why the viewer should care;
reflect the actual content of the video;
give enough context to avoid confusion;
move quickly toward the core subject.
A pattern interrupt should be used only when it strengthens the message.
Possible pattern interrupts include:
an unexpected visual contrast;
a result shown before the explanation;
a short cold-open scene;
a surprising but verified fact;
a direct demonstration;
an unusual question;
a brief contradiction;
a strong visual change.
Do not use random sound effects, shouting, unrelated footage, or misleading statements merely to interrupt the viewer’s attention.
Avoid hooks such as:
“You won’t believe this”
“Nobody is talking about this”
“This changes everything”
“Watch until the end”
“The secret they don’t want you to know”
“I almost didn’t make this video”
“99% of people get this wrong” unless verified;
“This will change your life”
“Before this video gets deleted”
INTRODUCTION
After the hook, provide only the context the viewer needs.
The introduction may include:
a brief explanation of what the video will cover;
why the topic matters;
the creator’s relevant perspective;
how the video is structured;
a short channel identification when useful.
Do not automatically begin with:
“Hey everyone, welcome back to the channel.”
If a channel welcome is included, keep it brief and integrate it naturally after the viewer already understands the topic.
Do not spend a long time explaining what will be explained.
Do not repeat the title, hook, and video promise in three different ways.
MAIN STRUCTURE
Organize the main section according to the topic.
Each major section should have a clear purpose, such as:
establishing context;
explaining the main mechanism;
presenting evidence;
showing an example;
demonstrating a process;
addressing a misconception;
comparing alternatives;
exploring consequences;
answering objections;
providing practical steps;
drawing a conclusion.
Use the number of sections that the topic genuinely requires.
Each section should:


Introduce one meaningful question or idea.
Develop it with explanation, evidence, or demonstration.
Connect it to the central argument.
Transition naturally into the next section.
Do not repeat the same conclusion after every section.
Do not use artificial transition phrases such as:


“But wait, there’s more”
“Here’s where things get crazy”
“You won’t believe what happens next”
“Now, let’s dive deeper”
“This brings us to our next point”
Use specific transitions based on the logic of the topic.
RETENTION
Maintain attention through substance, not constant gimmicks.
Use retention devices only when useful:
open questions that are later answered;
examples;
demonstrations;
visual changes;
comparisons;
stakes;
unresolved contradictions;
short stories;
progress markers;
practical payoffs;
changes in pace.
Do not create artificial suspense around obvious information.
Do not insert a new hook every 20 seconds.
Do not repeatedly say that the most important part is coming later.
Reveal useful information throughout the video rather than withholding everything until the end.
SPOKEN DIALOGUE
Write exact spoken dialogue, not bullet-point notes.
The dialogue should:
sound natural when read aloud;
use clear, pronunciation-friendly sentences;
use contractions when appropriate;
vary sentence length and rhythm;
allow pauses for emphasis;
explain technical language;
avoid overly formal phrasing;
avoid long, nested sentences;
avoid excessive rhetorical questions;
match {{creatorRole}}, {{tone}}, and {{brandVoice}}.
Do not write like an academic article unless the channel intentionally uses that style.
Do not repeatedly address the audience as “guys,” “friends,” or “everyone” unless it matches the established voice.
TIMESTAMPS
Divide the script into approximate timestamped sections.
The timestamps must add up realistically to {{duration}} minutes.
For each section provide:
section title;
approximate time range;
spoken dialogue;
visual direction;
B-roll or graphic cues;
on-screen text when useful;
editing or audio notes.
Do not assign exact second-by-second timing to every sentence unless requested.
B-ROLL AND VISUAL CUES
Use visual cues that explain, demonstrate, or emotionally support the narration.
Possible visual elements include:
direct-to-camera footage;

For each cue, explain what should appear and why it supports the narration.
Do not write only:
[B-ROLL: relevant footage]
Do not suggest:
unavailable footage;
fabricated dashboards;
fake social posts;
invented news headlines;
unverified charts;
customer logos without permission;
misleading before-and-after visuals;
complex cinematic scenes that exceed {{productionLimitations}}.
Mark difficult or optional footage as:
[OPTIONAL B-ROLL: description]

Use on-screen text only for information that benefits from visual reinforcement, such as:
key terms;
short quotations;
dates;
numbers;
steps;
contrasts;
section labels;
definitions.
Keep most on-screen text concise.
Do not display full narration paragraphs on screen.
Use this format:

[ON-SCREEN SOURCE: abbreviated source]
CHAPTERS
After the script, provide suggested YouTube chapters based on the final structure.
Chapter titles should:
accurately describe each section;
use natural language;
avoid clickbait;
help viewers navigate;
begin at realistic timestamps.
Do not create chapters for sections shorter than necessary.
CTA STRATEGY
Use one primary CTA and, if necessary, one subtle secondary CTA.
The CTA should match {{goal}} and {{desiredAction}}.
Possible CTAs include:
subscribe for a clearly defined type of future content;
watch a related video;
comment with a specific experience or answer;
download a supplied resource;
visit a supplied page;
try a practical step;
join a newsletter;
explore a product or service transparently.
Do not automatically use:
“Like, subscribe, and hit the notification bell.”
A subscription CTA should explain what viewers will receive from the channel.
Example:
“If you want more practical breakdowns of how small online products are built, subscribe. That’s what this channel focuses on.”
Do not ask viewers to like, subscribe, comment, share, visit a link, and buy something all at once.
MID-VIDEO CTA
Include a mid-video CTA only when it fits naturally.
It must not interrupt the most important explanation.
It should be brief and connected to the value already delivered.
Do not add a mid-video CTA by default.
OUTRO
The outro should:
resolve the central question;
summarize the most important takeaway without repeating the entire video;
explain what the viewer should remember or do;
lead naturally into the CTA;
optionally recommend a related video.
Keep the outro concise.
Do not introduce an entirely new argument in the final section.
STYLE
Write in a natural, credible voice.
Avoid:
generic motivational speeches;
exaggerated reactions;
fake vulnerability;
forced controversy;
repeated cliffhangers;
excessive self-promotion;
artificial enthusiasm;
corporate jargon;
excessive metaphors;
constant jokes;
unnecessary apologies;
keyword stuffing;
repeating the title throughout the script.
Avoid common YouTube and AI phrases such as:
“Without further ado”
“Let’s dive in”
“Smash that like button”
“Make sure you watch until the end”
“This is a game-changer”
“Here’s the thing”
“Let that sink in”
“But it gets even crazier”
“Your future self will thank you”
“This one simple trick”
“In today’s fast-paced world”
“Whether you’re a beginner or an expert”
FINAL QUALITY CHECK
Before presenting the script, silently verify that:
the word count fits {{duration}};
the structure matches {{videoFormat}};
the hook accurately represents the video;
the introduction does not unnecessarily delay the value;
every major section advances the central message;
no fact, story, statistic, quote, or source was invented;
the dialogue sounds natural when spoken;
visual cues are specific and achievable;
B-roll supports rather than distracts from the narration;
timestamps are realistic;
the conclusion resolves the video’s main promise;
the CTA is relevant and not overloaded;
the script does not sound like a generic AI-generated YouTube video.
