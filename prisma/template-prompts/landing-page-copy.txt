GOAL
Write a landing page that helps the right visitor quickly understand:

what the product is;
who it is for;
what problem it helps address;
how it works;
what makes it different;
which outcomes are supported by evidence;
what limitations or requirements matter;
what the visitor should do next.
The page should persuade through clarity, relevance, specificity, and credible proof rather than hype, pressure, or exaggerated promises.
ACCURACY AND CLAIMS


Use only facts, features, benefits, prices, metrics, timelines, testimonials, policies, and results supplied in the prompt.
Do not invent customer numbers, revenue results, time savings, conversion rates, awards, certifications, integrations, security claims, guarantees, or performance outcomes.
Do not turn a feature into a guaranteed benefit unless the connection is supported.
Do not claim that the product eliminates a problem completely unless that is proven.
Do not claim that every visitor experiences {{painPoint}}.
Present pain points as relevant possibilities or common challenges, not as assumptions about the individual reader.
Do not use phrases such as “you’re wasting money,” “your current process is broken,” or “you’re falling behind” unless explicitly supported and appropriate.
Do not claim that setup is instant, easy, effortless, or seamless unless confirmed.
Do not invent a free trial, demo, discount, deadline, limited availability, refund, or guarantee.
Do not imply that an offer is exclusive or personalized unless confirmed.
Do not fabricate customer quotations, names, logos, case studies, ratings, or review counts.
Clearly distinguish confirmed outcomes from possible benefits.
For medical, financial, legal, security, employment, housing, education, or other high-stakes products, use careful wording and avoid guaranteed outcomes.
AUDIENCE AND POSITIONING
Write specifically for {{audienceSegment}} at the stated {{awarenessStage}}.
Adapt the page accordingly:


Unaware audience:
  Begin with the recognizable problem or situation without immediately pushing the product.
Problem-aware audience:
  Clarify the cost or difficulty of the current approach, then introduce a better method.
Solution-aware audience:
  Explain why this product differs from other available solutions.
Product-aware audience:
  Focus on evidence, objections, requirements, pricing, and the next step.
Most-aware audience:
  Make the offer and CTA clear without unnecessary education.
Do not explain basic concepts the audience is already expected to understand.
Do not use technical terminology without explanation when writing for beginners.
CORE MESSAGE
Build the page around one primary value proposition:
{{confirmedBenefits}}
Do not try to present every product feature as equally important.
Choose one main outcome and support it with secondary benefits.
Keep the distinction clear:
Feature: what the product includes or does.
Benefit: how that capability helps the user.
Outcome: the measurable or observable result, only when supported.

Create:


Headline
Subheadline
Primary CTA button label
Optional supporting line below the CTA, only when useful
Headline requirements:


clearly communicate the primary value;
identify the product category or use case when needed;
be understandable without the tagline;
avoid vague slogans;
avoid unsupported superlatives;
avoid trying to mention every feature;
prioritize clarity over wordplay.
Subheadline requirements:
explain what the product does;
identify who it is for when useful;
clarify how the promised value is delivered;
include an important differentiator when supported;
avoid simply repeating the headline.
CTA requirements:
match {{desiredAction}};
clearly state the next step;
use approximately 2–5 words;
avoid vague labels when a specific label is possible.
Prefer CTA labels such as:
“Start the free trial”
“Request a demo”
“See how it works”
“Create your first project”
“View available plans”
“Download the guide”
“Check compatibility”
Avoid:
“Click here”
“Submit”
“Unlock success”
“Transform your business”
“Get amazing results”
Do not use “Start free” unless a free option is confirmed.
PROBLEM SECTION
Write a meaningful section heading and three concise pain-point bullets only when three distinct problems were supplied.
Each bullet should:
describe a recognizable situation;
explain the practical consequence;
use respectful, non-judgmental wording;
connect to a real user need;
avoid emotional manipulation.
Do not repeat the same problem using three different phrases.
Do not intensify the visitor’s fear, insecurity, or frustration solely to increase conversions.
If fewer than three distinct pain points were supplied, use only the supported number.
SOLUTION SECTION
Create up to three benefit blocks.
Each block should contain:
a clear benefit-focused heading;
a concise explanation;
the supporting feature or method;
an outcome only when confirmed.
Each benefit block must address a different audience need.
Do not make all headings variations of:
“Save time”
“Work smarter”
“Get better results”
Avoid benefit claims such as:
“Increase revenue”
“Boost productivity”
“Reduce costs”
“Grow faster”
unless they are supported or described as possible, context-dependent benefits.
HOW IT WORKS
Explain the real user journey in up to three steps.
Each step should contain:
a short action-oriented heading;
what the user does;
what the product does;
what happens next.
Use the actual onboarding or workflow supplied in {{howItWorks}}.
Do not invent integrations, setup steps, imports, automation, approvals, or implementation timelines.
Do not describe the process as three steps when the real process is more complex. In that case, summarize the three major stages and clearly indicate that additional setup may be required.
SOCIAL PROOF
Use only approved proof.
Possible proof formats include:
verified customer quote;
confirmed metric;
approved client logo list;
short case study result;
verified review rating;
certification or trust signal.
If no approved proof was supplied, use an explicit placeholder:
[Insert approved customer quote, verified metric, case study, or trust signal here]
Do not write a fictional testimonial.
Do not create placeholder text that looks like a real quote.
Do not invent a customer name, role, company, metric, rating, or logo.
OBJECTION HANDLING
Include a short objection section when {{objections}} contains meaningful concerns.
Address concerns such as:
setup time;
technical ability;
pricing;
security;
data ownership;
compatibility;
cancellation;
implementation effort;
suitability for a specific team or use case.
Answer objections honestly.
Do not dismiss concerns with phrases such as:
“There’s nothing to worry about”
“It couldn’t be easier”
“Anyone can do it”
“No risk whatsoever”
Mention limitations, requirements, or conditions where relevant.
FINAL CTA
Create a final section containing:


A concise headline
One supporting sentence
One primary CTA button label
Optional reassurance based only on confirmed details
The final CTA should:


reinforce the main value;
clarify the next step;
avoid introducing a new offer;
avoid false urgency;
avoid making the visitor feel guilty for not acting;
use the same primary action as the hero unless there is a strategic reason not to.
Do not add countdowns, deadlines, scarcity, or “limited spots” unless confirmed.
STYLE
Write in a clear, natural, confident voice appropriate for {{audience}} and {{brandVoice}}.
Use plain language, varied sentence lengths, and concise paragraphs.
Make every section easy to scan on mobile.
Prefer concrete language over abstract marketing terminology.
Avoid:
vague transformation promises;
excessive adjectives;
repeated product names;
keyword stuffing;
excessive exclamation marks;
unnecessary emojis;
corporate jargon;
fake familiarity;
forced emotional storytelling;
excessive rhetorical questions;
repeating the CTA after every section.
Avoid common landing page and AI phrases such as:
“In today’s fast-paced world”
“The all-in-one solution”
“Revolutionize the way you work”
“Take your business to the next level”
“Unlock your full potential”
“Game-changing”
“Powerful and intuitive”
“Seamless experience”
“Say goodbye to”
“Say hello to”
“Built for everyone”
“Everything you need”
“Results that speak for themselves”
“Join thousands of happy customers” unless confirmed.
SEO AND MESSAGE CONSISTENCY
When relevant:


Use the primary topic or product category naturally in the headline, subheadline, or early page copy.
Do not stuff keywords into benefit headings.
Ensure that the page copy matches the promise made by the ad, search result, email, or social post sending traffic to the page.
Do not introduce claims on the landing page that were not present or supported in the source campaign.
Keep terminology consistent across the hero, features, workflow, and CTA.
FINAL QUALITY CHECK
Before presenting the page, silently verify that:


the product is understandable within the hero section;
the audience and use case are clear;
the page focuses on one primary value proposition;
no feature, metric, quote, price, policy, or result was invented;
the pain section does not manipulate or insult the reader;
every benefit is connected to a supplied feature or method;
the workflow reflects the actual product;
no fictional social proof appears;
limitations and requirements are not hidden;
the CTA clearly describes the next step;
the same action is used consistently;
the page is readable on mobile;
the copy does not sound like a generic AI landing page.
