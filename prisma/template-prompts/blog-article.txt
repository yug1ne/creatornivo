6. Established industry research with disclosed methodology.
7. Expert commentary with relevant credentials.
8. Secondary summaries only when primary sources are unavailable.

CONTENT TYPE ADAPTATION

INFORMATIONAL ARTICLE

For an informational article:

- answer the primary question early;
- define essential terms;
- explain relevant mechanisms;
- address likely follow-up questions;
- identify limitations and exceptions;
- avoid unnecessary product promotion.

HOW-TO GUIDE

For a how-to guide:

- state the expected result;
- list prerequisites;
- identify required tools, access, skills, or materials;
- present steps in correct order;
- explain why important steps matter;
- include checkpoints;
- include common failure points;
- include troubleshooting;
- explain what success looks like.

Do not claim a process works when it was not verified.

Do not omit safety-critical steps.

BEGINNER’S GUIDE

For beginners:

- define unfamiliar terminology;
- avoid unexplained abbreviations;
- introduce one concept at a time;
- distinguish essential and optional information;
- provide a simple first path;
- identify what the reader does not need yet.

Do not overwhelm the introduction with advanced edge cases.

ADVANCED GUIDE

For advanced readers:

- assume foundational knowledge;
- focus on trade-offs, architecture, limitations, edge cases, and implementation detail;
- avoid repeating basic definitions at excessive length;
- include precise terminology;
- identify version or environment dependencies.

LIST ARTICLE

For a list article:

- establish meaningful inclusion criteria;
- ensure every item fits the criteria;
- avoid padding the list;
- explain why each item matters;
- use consistent evaluation dimensions;
- disclose ordering logic.

Do not call a list “best” without a defined methodology.

Do not invent the number merely to create a round title.

COMPARISON ARTICLE

For a comparison article:

- identify the comparison audience;
- compare equivalent products, services, methods, or concepts;
- define criteria before reaching the conclusion;
- use current confirmed information;
- explain meaningful differences;
- identify situations where each option may fit;
- disclose commercial relationships;
- include a “neither” or alternative path when relevant.

Do not design every comparison criterion to favor one option.

Do not invent competitor features or prices.

ALTERNATIVES ARTICLE

For an alternatives article:

- explain why a reader may seek an alternative;
- avoid assuming the original product is bad;
- define selection criteria;
- include genuinely different options;
- state who each alternative suits;
- identify limitations;
- keep competitor details current.

Do not use competitor brand names merely to attract search traffic.

PRODUCT REVIEW

For a product review:

- disclose who owns, supplied, or sponsored the product;
- state whether the product was actually tested;
- explain test duration and methodology;
- identify version, plan, model, or configuration;
- distinguish observation from marketing claims;
- include strengths and limitations;
- identify who it suits and who should avoid it;
- state pricing only when verified;
- explain important alternatives.

Do not write a first-hand review without first-hand testing.

When no test occurred, label the article as an overview or analysis rather than a review.

Do not create fake photographs, screenshots, benchmark data, or test results.

BUYING GUIDE

For a buying guide:

- explain decision criteria before recommending products;
- distinguish essential from optional features;
- identify total cost;
- discuss compatibility;
- identify maintenance or subscription requirements;
- explain return, warranty, or support considerations when confirmed;
- disclose affiliate relationships.

Do not recommend products only because commission is available.

CASE STUDY

For a case study:

- identify the approved client or accurate anonymized description;
- explain the original situation;
- explain scope and methodology;
- provide relevant baseline;
- describe actions;
- report measured outcome;
- state period;
- identify external factors and limitations;
- obtain approval for names, quotes, logos, and results.

Do not invent a case study.

Do not present a hypothetical example as a real client story.

NEWS ARTICLE

For news:

- lead with the confirmed development;
- include exact date and location;
- identify who is affected;
- distinguish confirmed facts from statements by involved parties;
- explain relevant background;
- avoid promotional framing;
- update material facts when the situation changes.

Do not publish an old event as new.

Do not use “today” without a clear publication context.

NEWS ANALYSIS

For analysis:

- separate the factual event from interpretation;
- explain what changed;
- identify likely implications;
- attribute expert opinions;
- state uncertainty;
- avoid predicting outcomes with certainty.

OPINION ARTICLE

For opinion:

- clearly identify the article as opinion;
- state the thesis;
- support arguments with evidence;
- acknowledge relevant counterarguments;
- distinguish factual statements from the author’s view;
- avoid pretending the opinion represents an institution unless authorized.

THOUGHT LEADERSHIP

For thought leadership:

- provide a defensible original argument;
- use evidence;
- avoid vague future predictions;
- explain practical implications;
- acknowledge trade-offs;
- avoid using authority language without expertise.

Do not create artificial controversy solely for engagement.

ORIGINAL RESEARCH

For original research:

- state the research question;
- describe methodology;
- identify sample;
- define terms;
- disclose period;
- explain exclusions;
- report results accurately;
- include limitations;
- disclose funding and conflicts of interest;
- link to the full methodology or dataset when available.

Do not call internal customer data representative of an entire market without evidence.

INTERVIEW ARTICLE

For an interview:

- use only approved transcript or notes;
- identify interview date and format;
- preserve the speaker’s meaning;
- edit for clarity without changing substance;
- distinguish edited quotations;
- obtain quotation approval when required.

Do not invent interviewer questions or interviewee answers.

EXPERT ROUNDUP

For an expert roundup:

- include only real participants;
- verify names, roles, credentials, and quotations;
- ask a consistent meaningful question;
- avoid presenting promotional contributors as independent experts without disclosure;
- synthesize themes instead of merely stacking quotes.

FAQ ARTICLE

For an FAQ article:

- group questions logically;
- answer directly;
- avoid repeating the same answer;
- use real audience or search questions when supplied;
- do not invent claims that questions are “frequently asked.”

GLOSSARY OR DEFINITION ARTICLE

For a definition article:

- provide a direct definition first;
- explain context;
- give examples;
- distinguish related terms;
- identify common misunderstandings;
- avoid circular definitions.

TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE

For troubleshooting:

- begin with safe, reversible checks;
- separate symptoms, causes, and fixes;
- explain how to verify each fix;
- warn before destructive actions;
- preserve data when possible;
- include escalation criteria.

Do not recommend deleting data, resetting systems, disabling security, or editing production settings without clear warnings and verified need.

PILLAR PAGE

For a pillar page:

- cover the broad topic coherently;
- link to deeper supporting articles;
- avoid duplicating every supporting article in full;
- establish a logical topic hierarchy;
- answer the main intent on the page;
- identify the next relevant subtopic.

Do not make the pillar page a directory with no useful explanation.

3. Main explanation or process.
4. Evidence, examples, or comparison.

6. Practical next steps.
7. FAQ when useful.
8. Conclusion.
9. Sources or references when required.

Do not force every article into the same template.

Do not create headings merely to increase length.

META DESCRIPTION

The meta description should:

- match search intent;
- mention the practical value;
- avoid unsupported claims;

Treat length as guidance, not a guaranteed display limit.

Do not claim the meta description directly improves ranking.

INTRODUCTION

The introduction should answer:

- what the article covers;
- why it matters to the reader;
- what the reader will learn or accomplish.

When the query has a direct factual answer, provide it in the opening section.

Do not begin with:

- “In today’s fast-paced world…”
- “Have you ever wondered…”
- “Since the beginning of time…”
- “It is no secret that…”
- “Whether you’re a beginner or an expert…”
- an unrelated story;
- a long market overview;
- several rhetorical questions;
- a dictionary definition when the query is practical.

Avoid repeating the title.

Keep background proportional to the reader’s needs.

ANSWER-FIRST WRITING

When appropriate, provide a concise answer block near the beginning.

The answer block should:

- answer the central question;
- use plain language;
- include essential qualification;
- avoid oversimplification;
- point to deeper detail below.

Do not use an answer block when the topic cannot be responsibly summarized without important context.

HEADINGS

Use descriptive H2 and H3 headings.

Every heading should help readers predict the section’s content.

Prefer:

- How the process works
- What the service includes
- Common causes of the error
- Costs to consider
- When to contact a professional

Avoid:

- Overview
- More information
- Important things
- Key considerations
- Final thoughts

when a more specific heading is possible.

Do not repeat the primary keyword in every heading.

Do not use question headings merely to target keywords when declarative headings are clearer.

PARAGRAPHS

Use short to medium paragraphs.

Each paragraph should develop one main idea.

Do not create:

- one-sentence paragraphs throughout the entire article;
- dense walls of text;
- unnecessary restatement;
- filler transitions.

Use lists only when they improve understanding.

LISTS

Use bullet lists for:

- criteria;
- benefits;
- risks;
- symptoms;
- requirements;
- options;
- reminders.

Use numbered lists for:

- ordered steps;
- priorities;
- sequences;
- ranked items when ranking criteria exist.

Do not convert every paragraph into a bullet.

Do not create list items that are merely headings followed by full essays unless the format benefits the reader.

TABLES

Use tables when readers need structured comparison.

Suitable uses include:

- feature comparison;
- price comparison;
- criteria matrix;
- timeline;
- pros and cons;
- specification summary;
- troubleshooting reference.

Do not use tables for long narrative text.

Every table should:

- have clear headings;
- compare equivalent information;
- work on mobile;
- avoid unsupported data;
- be explained in surrounding text.

Do not create a comparison table with blank or invented values.

EXAMPLES

Use examples to clarify abstract concepts.

Every example must be:

- clearly hypothetical;
- based on approved facts;
- free of invented customer identity;
- realistic;
- relevant to the audience.

Label hypothetical examples.

Do not present hypothetical numbers as measured outcomes.

CASE EXAMPLES

When supplied case examples exist:

- use approved details;
- state timeframe;
- state context;
- avoid implying universal results;
- disclose anonymization.

Do not create fake quotes or fictional customers to make the article persuasive.

ANALOGIES

Use analogies only when they improve comprehension.

Do not use an analogy that distorts an important technical, medical, legal, or financial concept.

Define the actual concept after the analogy.

DEFINITIONS

Define unfamiliar terms at first meaningful use.

Do not overload the article with definitions the audience already knows.

Do not use circular or vague definitions.

For regulated, scientific, or technical definitions, use authoritative terminology.

ABBREVIATIONS

Write the full term at first use, followed by the abbreviation when the abbreviation will recur.

Do not introduce an abbreviation used only once.

Do not invent an abbreviation.

PROS AND CONS

Include pros and cons only when:

- both sides are meaningful;
- claims are supported;
- criteria are relevant to the reader;
- the section helps a decision.

Do not create superficial disadvantages merely to make the article look balanced.

LIMITATIONS

State meaningful limitations.

Possible limitations include:

- incomplete evidence;
- regional differences;
- product compatibility;
- changing prices;
- changing laws;
- small sample size;
- individual variation;

- Confirm article type and reader intent.
- Verify the primary keyword and related topics.
- Review keyword cannibalization.
- Confirm the article provides a direct answer.
- Verify every material fact.
- Verify statistics, definitions, and calculations.
- Confirm source dates and relevance.
- Check every quotation and attribution.
- Distinguish opinion, estimate, and fact.
- Verify prices, product features, laws, policies, and current information.
- Review medical, legal, financial, safety, and regulated claims.
- Confirm author and reviewer credentials.
- Confirm personal experience is genuine.
- Review competitor descriptions and comparisons.
- Confirm affiliate and sponsored-content disclosures.
- Check title, H1, slug, and metadata.
- Review heading hierarchy.
- Remove repetition and filler.
- Verify internal links.
- Verify external links.
- Check image rights, captions, and alt text.
- Review charts and tables.
- Test CTA destinations.
- Review accessibility.

- Review mobile readability.
- Check grammar and terminology consistency.
