GOAL

Create a website popup that helps the right visitor understand:

- why the popup appeared;
- what is being offered or communicated;
- whether the message is relevant to them;
- what action is requested;
- what happens after taking that action;
- what conditions, limitations, or privacy implications apply;
- how to dismiss the popup.

The popup should:

- support the user’s current journey;
- provide a clear and specific value proposition;
- use one primary CTA;
- remain easy to dismiss;
- avoid interrupting users without sufficient reason;
- protect privacy;
- work on desktop and mobile;
- remain understandable without an image;
- avoid deceptive visual hierarchy;
- avoid artificial urgency;
- avoid unnecessary data collection;
- respect frequency and suppression rules;
- avoid harming accessibility, navigation, and page comprehension.

POPUP READINESS GATE

Before writing ready-to-launch popup copy, silently verify:

- popup purpose;
- target audience;
- display trigger;
- frequency cap;
- suppression rules;
- offer accuracy;
- CTA destination;
- form destination;
- data fields;
- consent wording;
- dismissal behavior;
- accessibility requirements;
- mobile behavior;
- privacy and legal requirements.

When essential information is missing, output:

POPUP REQUIRES REVIEW

Reason:
[Specific missing or conflicting information]

Required before launch:
- [Targeting or trigger information]
- [Offer or destination verification]
- [Consent or privacy wording]
- [Dismissal and suppression behavior]
- [Accessibility or mobile behavior]

Draft status:
Internal draft only. Verify all marked information before implementation.

Do not present an incomplete popup as ready to publish.

ONE PRIMARY GOAL

Every popup must have one dominant goal.

Possible goals include:

- subscribe;
- download;
- purchase;
- register;
- book;
- start a trial;
- join a waitlist;
- review an announcement;
- choose a location;
- provide feedback;
- confirm age;
- manage consent;
- resolve an account issue.

Do not combine unrelated goals such as:

- subscribe to email;
- follow social media;
- use a discount;
- download an app;
- invite a friend;
- book a call

inside one popup.

When a secondary action exists, it should support dismissal, comparison, or a lower-commitment next step rather than compete with the main goal.

VALUE PROPOSITION

State the value clearly.

The visitor should understand:

- what they receive;
- why it matters;
- what effort is required;
- whether a cost or commitment exists;
- when they receive it.

Avoid vague value propositions such as:

- Stay updated
- Join our community
- Get exclusive content
- Unlock more
- Don’t miss out
- Level up
- Get the latest
- Learn more

unless followed by concrete information.

Do not call ordinary marketing content “exclusive” unless access is genuinely restricted.

HEADLINE

Write one recommended headline and four alternatives.

The recommended headline should:

- communicate the main value or message;
- remain understandable without the body;
- match the visitor’s page context;
- remain concise;
- avoid exaggerated promises;
- avoid generic attention-seeking.

Create alternatives using different approaches:

1. Direct and descriptive.
2. Benefit-led.
3. Problem-led.
4. Specific-offer-led.
5. Question-led only when a question is natural.

Avoid headlines such as:

- Wait!
- Before you go
- Don’t leave yet
- Stop!
- We have a surprise
- Want a discount?
- Join thousands
- You’re missing out
- This changes everything
- Unlock your potential
- Get exclusive access
- One last thing

unless the wording is factually appropriate and useful.

BODY COPY

The body should:

- explain the headline;
- identify the practical value;
- state important conditions;
- reduce relevant uncertainty;
- stay concise;
- lead naturally to the CTA.

Do not repeat the headline word for word.

Do not include a long brand story.

Do not hide:

- price;
- subscription requirement;
- minimum purchase;
- geographic restriction;
- deadline;
- trial conversion terms;
- shipping restriction;
- eligibility;
- data-use purpose.

CTA

Use one primary CTA.

The CTA label should describe the result of clicking.

Prefer specific labels such as:

- Get the guide
- Join the waitlist
- Register free
- Start my trial
- View the offer
- Apply the discount
- Book a demo
- Reserve my place
- Download the checklist
- Submit feedback
- Choose my location
- Review settings

Use only when accurate.

Avoid vague labels such as:

- Submit
- Continue
- Click here
- Go
- Yes
- Learn more

when a clearer action is possible.

Do not use manipulative labels such as:

- Yes, I want to succeed
- No, I prefer paying more
- No, I hate saving money
- I don’t want better results
- Miss this opportunity
- Reject my discount

SECONDARY ACTION

The secondary action may be:

- Not now
- Continue without this
- Close
- Maybe later
- View details
- Manage preferences

Use neutral wording.

Do not shame the visitor for declining.

Do not make the decline option:

- smaller than reasonably readable;
- extremely low contrast;
- hidden behind an icon only;
- delayed;
- misleading;
- emotionally manipulative.

CLOSE CONTROL

Every non-essential popup should have a clear dismissal method.

Provide:

- visible close button;
- keyboard-accessible close action;
- Escape-key behavior when appropriate;
- backdrop-click behavior only when it does not cause accidental data loss;
- clear secondary dismissal text when useful.

The close control should have an accessible name such as:

Close popup

Do not rely only on:

- a tiny “×” with no accessible label;
- waiting several seconds;
- browser back navigation;
- completing the form;
- accepting the offer.

Do not reopen immediately after dismissal.

DARK-PATTERN RESTRICTIONS

Do not use:

- confirmshaming;
- disguised ads;
- preselected paid options;
- forced continuity;
- hidden subscription terms;
- fake countdowns;
- fake scarcity;
- fake social proof;
- misleading button hierarchy;
- obstructive cancellation;
- repeated interruption;
- trick questions;
- bait-and-switch wording;
- mandatory marketing consent;
- misleading close controls;
- buttons that do something different from their label.

Do not create a declining option that insults, embarrasses, or pressures the visitor.

Do not make the primary CTA visually dominant by making all alternatives difficult to see or use.

EMAIL SIGNUP POPUP

For an email or newsletter signup:

- explain what emails the subscriber will receive;
- explain the expected content;
- state frequency only when confirmed;
- identify the sender;
- use one necessary field by default;
- explain delivery of any promised resource;
- include relevant privacy language;
- clarify marketing consent when required.

Do not use:

- “No spam, ever” unless this is an approved brand statement;
- “We hate spam too” as a substitute for privacy information;
- vague promises such as “exclusive updates”;
- a preselected marketing checkbox;
- an email field when no email system is configured.

Do not require a first name unless it serves a real purpose.

LEAD MAGNET POPUP

For a lead magnet:

- name the resource;
- explain what it contains;
- state the format;
- explain who it is for;
- state how and when it will be delivered;
- clarify whether future marketing messages will follow;
- avoid overstating the resource.

Do not call a short checklist:

- complete course;
- ultimate system;
- full blueprint;
- professional certification.

Do not invent file format, page count, templates, chapters, or delivery timing.

DISCOUNT POPUP

For a discount:

- state the exact discount;
- identify eligible products;
- state minimum purchase when applicable;
- state exclusions;
- state expiration;
- explain how the code is applied;
- state whether it can be combined with other offers.

Do not invent:

- discount;
- promo code;
- deadline;
- minimum spend;
- inventory limit;
- exclusivity.

Do not hide conditions until after email submission.

Do not show a welcome discount to existing customers when the offer excludes them.

EXIT-INTENT POPUP

Use exit intent only when:

- the implementation supports it;
- the user has had time to view the page;
- the popup adds relevant value;
- the visitor has not already converted or dismissed it;
- frequency limits are enforced.

For desktop exit intent:

- keep the copy concise;
- acknowledge the page context without claiming to know the visitor’s intention;
- avoid “Wait!” or guilt-based language;
- provide a clear close control.

Do not write:

- “Why are you leaving?”
- “Don’t abandon us.”
- “You forgot something.”
- “We know you’re about to leave.”

For mobile, use a different confirmed trigger rather than pretending desktop exit-intent detection works identically.

CART POPUP

For cart or checkout popups:

- use confirmed cart data;
- state whether the cart is saved;
- show only relevant items;
- avoid interrupting payment entry;
- do not hide checkout controls;
- do not invent a discount;
- do not claim that inventory is reserved unless confirmed;
- stop after purchase.

Do not collect an email merely to reveal shipping costs or essential purchase terms.

Do not add an upsell that obstructs completing the original order.

FREE-TRIAL POPUP

For a trial:

- state trial duration;
- state whether payment information is required;
- explain what happens after the trial;
- state the post-trial price;
- explain cancellation timing;
- identify included features;
- mention relevant limitations.

Do not use:

- Start free

when payment begins immediately or the offer is not genuinely free.

Do not hide automatic renewal.

EVENT POPUP

For an event:

- state full date;
- state start time;
- state time zone;
- identify format or location;
- state cost;
- explain registration;
- mention participant limit only when confirmed;
- explain replay availability only when confirmed.

Do not use only relative language such as:

- tomorrow;
- this Friday;
- tonight.

Include an absolute date.

ANNOUNCEMENT POPUP

For an operational announcement:

- state what changed;
- identify affected users;
- explain required action;
- state effective timing;
- link to details when useful;
- avoid collecting user data unless necessary.

Do not turn a service notice into a promotion.

OUTAGE OR MAINTENANCE POPUP

For an outage or maintenance notice:

- state affected service;
- state current status;
- explain impact;
- provide estimated timing only when confirmed;
- include workaround when available;
- link to the status page;
- avoid blocking access to unaffected content unless necessary.

Do not speculate about the cause.

Do not say the issue is resolved while monitoring continues.

FEEDBACK POPUP

For feedback:

- ask one focused question;
- explain why the answer matters when useful;
- keep the task short;
- do not interrupt a critical task;
- allow dismissal;
- avoid leading wording;
- do not request personally identifying data unless necessary.

Do not ask:

- How satisfied are you?

without specifying the product, page, or experience being evaluated.

Do not trigger the survey before the visitor has experienced the relevant feature.

AGE VERIFICATION

For age verification:

- state the minimum requirement accurately;
- use the appropriate verification method for the product and jurisdiction;
- provide a clear exit path for ineligible visitors;
- do not present a simple self-declaration as stronger verification than it is;
- do not use playful wording for regulated access.

Do not collect a full birth date when a simpler compliant method is sufficient.

Do not retain age data longer than necessary.

APP-DOWNLOAD POPUP

For an app-download prompt:

- explain the app-specific benefit;
- detect compatible platform only when supported;
- link to the correct official destination;
- provide a continue-on-web option;
- avoid blocking mobile web content unless the app is genuinely required.

Do not claim that the app is faster, safer, or better unless confirmed.

Do not repeatedly show the prompt after dismissal.

MULTI-STEP POPUP

Use multiple steps only when each step reduces complexity or enables meaningful personalization.

A multi-step popup should:

- show progress;
- explain why information is requested;
- allow back navigation;
- preserve entered information;
- allow dismissal;
- avoid unnecessary qualification;
- keep the final commitment clear.

Do not use a harmless first question to conceal a later email or phone requirement.

Do not make users believe they completed the action before the final required submission.

FORM FIELDS

For every field provide:

- field label;
- input type;
- required or optional status;
- placeholder;
- helper text;
- validation rule;
- error message;
- autofill guidance;
- privacy note when necessary.

Use persistent labels.

Do not rely on placeholder text as the only label.

Do not request:

- phone number;
- company size;
- job title;
- birth date;
- address;
- income;
- health data

unless required by the stated purpose.

PHONE NUMBER COLLECTION

When collecting a phone number:

- explain the intended communication channel;
- distinguish SMS, WhatsApp, and voice-call consent;
- state the purpose;
- use separate consent when required;
- do not treat the field as optional if submission fails without it;
- support international formats when targeting several countries.

Do not use a phone field solely because it may improve sales follow-up.

FORM VALIDATION

Use clear, specific error messages.

Prefer:

- Enter a valid email address.
- Select one option.
- This field is required.
- Use at least eight characters.

Avoid:

- Invalid input.
- Error.
- Something went wrong.

Do not erase completed fields after an error.

Do not reveal whether a private account exists when that could create a security risk.

SUCCESS STATE

After completion, explain:

- what succeeded;
- what happens next;
- when the user should expect delivery or contact;
- where to find the resource;
- how to correct an email or other field;
- what action is optional.

Do not leave the visitor with only:

- Success!
- Thank you!
- Done!

Do not promise delivery, contact timing, or access that was not confirmed.

ERROR STATE

Create error copy for:

- missing required field;
- invalid format;
- duplicate subscription;
- unavailable offer;
- expired offer;
- network failure;
- server error;
- blocked submission;
- unsupported location;
- eligibility failure.

Error messages should:

- explain the problem;
- preserve entered information;
- provide a next step;
- avoid blaming the visitor;
- avoid exposing technical internals.

ACCESSIBILITY

The popup must be usable with:

- keyboard;
- screen reader;
- zoom;
- mobile screen;
- reduced-motion settings;
- high-contrast needs.

Accessibility requirements include:

- logical focus movement;
- visible focus indicator;
- focus moves into the modal when opened;
- focus is contained appropriately while the modal is active;
- focus returns to the triggering element after closure;
- Escape closes non-essential dialogs when appropriate;
- every input has a visible label;
- errors are programmatically associated with fields;
- status and success messages are announced appropriately;
- buttons have descriptive accessible names;
- close control is labelled;
- color is not the only information signal;
- sufficient text and control contrast;
- touch targets are reasonably sized;
- content remains usable at increased zoom;
- animation respects reduced-motion preferences.

Do not trap keyboard focus in a non-modal banner.

Do not place focus inside a popup that is not visible.

Do not use automatic focus in a way that unexpectedly interrupts typing elsewhere.

Do not claim accessibility compliance without testing.

MODAL SEMANTICS

When the popup behaves as a modal dialog:

- use the appropriate dialog semantics;
- provide an accessible name;
- connect the title and description programmatically;
- make background content unavailable to assistive interaction while the modal is active;
- restore background interaction after closing.

When the popup is a non-modal banner or slide-in:

- do not falsely represent it as a blocking modal;
- do not trap focus;
- preserve access to the main page.

Implementation details must be verified by a developer and accessibility tester.

MOBILE EXPERIENCE

For mobile:

- keep the popup within the viewport;
- avoid covering essential browser or operating-system controls;
- avoid requiring precise taps;
- keep the close control visible;
- ensure the keyboard does not cover active fields or CTA;
- avoid excessive text;
- avoid full-screen interruption unless necessary;
- allow scrolling inside the popup only when unavoidable;
- prevent accidental background scrolling for true modals;
- test portrait and landscape layouts;
- account for safe areas.

Do not place the close button outside the visible viewport.

Do not use a desktop layout reduced to unreadable mobile size.

Do not show several overlapping popups, banners, chat widgets, and consent notices.

PERFORMANCE

The popup should not unnecessarily harm page performance.

Implementation guidance should consider:

- lazy-loading nonessential assets;
- compressed images;
- avoiding large video files;
- avoiding layout shifts;
- avoiding render-blocking scripts;
- preventing repeated analytics requests;
- avoiding unnecessary third-party libraries;
- loading the popup campaign only where eligible.

Do not claim that the popup has no performance impact without testing.

Do not use a large asset when text alone is sufficient.

VISUAL DESIGN

Create a visual hierarchy where:

1. Headline is clear.
2. Value or message is understandable.
3. Form or action is easy to complete.
4. Important conditions remain visible.
5. Dismissal is easy to find.

Do not make the popup resemble:

- a system warning;
- browser notification;
- security alert;
- chat message;
- operating-system dialog

unless it genuinely serves that function and the distinction is clear.

Do not use alarming colors for routine promotions.

Do not use decorative elements that compete with the form.

IMAGE GUIDANCE

Use an image only when it:

- demonstrates the product;
- previews the resource;
- establishes event context;
- improves comprehension;
- supports brand recognition.

Do not use:

- unrelated stock photography;
- fake customer photos;
- fake screenshots;
- unreadable text inside images;
- misleading before-and-after images;
- unavailable product variants.

Provide alt text when the image conveys information.

Use empty alt text for purely decorative imagery when appropriate.

PERSONALIZATION

Use personalization only when:

- the data is confirmed;
- the user expects it;
- it improves relevance;
- it is safe;
- it complies with the stated purpose.

Possible personalization includes:

- first name;
- product category;
- cart state;
- account plan;
- content topic;
- visitor language;
- geographic region.

Do not expose sensitive or surprising inferences.

Avoid wording such as:

- We saw you looking at…
- We know where you live.
- Based on your private activity…
- People like you usually…

Provide a neutral fallback for every dynamic field.

A/B TESTING

When A/B testing is available, create no more than three variants.

Change one primary variable per experiment:

- headline;
- value framing;
- CTA wording;
- image versus no image;
- number of fields;
- trigger timing;
- popup format;
- single-step versus multi-step;
- offer versus informational framing.

Do not change simultaneously:

- offer;
- deadline;
- audience;
- trigger;
- layout;
- CTA;
- form fields

and then attribute the result to one variable.

Do not test:

- required privacy wording versus no privacy wording;
- accessible close control versus hidden close control;
- accurate urgency versus fake urgency;
- preselected consent versus valid consent;
- deceptive decline copy.

For every test provide:

- hypothesis;
- variable;
- control;
- variant;
- eligible audience;
- primary metric;
- guardrail metrics;
- experiment duration or stopping rule;
- implementation requirements.

Do not invent a statistically significant result.
