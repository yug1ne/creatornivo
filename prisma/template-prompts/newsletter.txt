## Newsletter Type Guidance

Adapt the structure and writing style to the selected newsletter type.

Possible newsletter types may include:

* Weekly or monthly digest
* Educational newsletter
* Industry insights newsletter
* Product or company update
* Founder newsletter
* Curated content newsletter
* Community newsletter
* Promotional newsletter
* Editorial newsletter
* Event newsletter
* Customer newsletter
* Personal creator newsletter

Do not force every newsletter into the same structure. Choose the format that best supports the stated goal, audience, and content.

## Core Requirements

Write a newsletter that:

* Provides clear value from the opening lines.
* Has one central theme rather than several disconnected topics.
* Uses a natural, confident, and audience-appropriate voice.
* Makes the reader understand why the topic matters.
* Is easy to scan on both desktop and mobile.
* Uses short paragraphs and clear section breaks.
* Avoids unnecessary introductions and filler.
* Maintains a logical flow from the opening to the final call to action.
* Balances useful information with personality.
* Includes specific observations, examples, explanations, or practical takeaways whenever relevant.
* Makes promotional content feel connected to the reader’s needs.
* Avoids exaggerated promises, fake urgency, and manipulative language.
* Does not invent statistics, testimonials, quotes, customer stories, research findings, product features, or company achievements.
* Clearly marks any missing factual information with a neutral placeholder instead of fabricating it.
* Avoids repetitive wording and generic AI phrases.
* Does not overuse bullet points, emojis, rhetorical questions, or exclamation marks.

## Subject Line Requirements

Provide 8 subject line options.

The subject lines should vary in approach:

1. Direct and informative
2. Curiosity-driven
3. Benefit-focused
4. Conversational
5. Insight-focused
6. Short and minimal
7. Question-based
8. Slightly unexpected but still relevant

Each subject line must:

* Match the actual newsletter content.
* Avoid misleading clickbait.
* Avoid spam-heavy wording.
* Avoid unnecessary capitalization.
* Remain clear when viewed on a mobile device.
* Preferably stay under 55 characters when possible.

Do not use false urgency, fake scarcity, or unsupported claims.

## Preview Text Requirements

Provide 5 preview text options.

Each preview text should:

* Complement rather than repeat the subject line.
* Add useful context or create a clear reason to open.
* Stay concise.
* Preferably remain under 90 characters.
* Avoid generic phrases such as “You don’t want to miss this.”

## Opening Requirements

Begin with a strong opening that immediately establishes relevance.

Depending on the newsletter type, the opening may use:

* A timely observation
* A relatable problem
* A useful insight
* A surprising contrast
* A brief personal note
* A direct summary of what the reader will learn
* A clear update from the company, creator, or community

Do not begin with generic phrases such as:

* “We hope this email finds you well.”
* “In today’s fast-paced world.”
* “We are excited to announce.”
* “Welcome to another edition.”
* “In this newsletter, we will explore.”

A welcome sentence may be used only when it adds personality or context.

## Main Content Structure

Choose the most suitable structure for the newsletter.

A recommended structure is:

### 1. Opening Hook

Introduce the main topic and explain why it matters to the reader.

### 2. Main Insight or Story

Develop the central idea using useful context, explanation, observations, or a short story.

### 3. Key Takeaways

Present the most important lessons, updates, or practical points.

Use bullets only when they improve readability.

### 4. Practical Application

Explain what the reader can do, consider, test, avoid, or explore next.

### 5. Secondary Section

Include one relevant supporting section when appropriate, such as:

* Quick tip
* Useful resource
* Product update
* Behind-the-scenes note
* Recommended article
* Community highlight
* Upcoming event
* Common mistake
* Question of the week
* Short curated list

Do not add a secondary section merely to make the newsletter longer.

### 6. Call to Action

End with one clear primary action.

The call to action should be specific and naturally connected to the newsletter content.

Examples of suitable actions include:

* Read the full article
* Try a feature
* Reply with an opinion
* Register for an event
* Download a resource
* Join a community
* View an update
* Complete a survey
* Explore a product
* Share the newsletter

Avoid placing several competing calls to action in the same section.

## Personalization and Audience Relevance

Adapt the newsletter to the target audience’s:

* Level of knowledge
* Main goals
* Common frustrations
* Likely objections
* Preferred language
* Relationship with the sender
* Stage of awareness

Do not assume that every reader already understands the topic, product, or industry.

Explain specialist terms briefly when necessary, but do not make the writing feel like a textbook.

## Tone and Style

Follow the requested tone of voice consistently.

Unless otherwise specified, the writing should be:

* Clear
* Useful
* Conversational
* Credible
* Warm without being overly informal
* Professional without sounding corporate
* Persuasive without applying pressure

Use contractions and natural sentence variation when appropriate for the selected language.

Avoid:

* Empty motivational language
* Corporate jargon
* Excessive hype
* Long blocks of text
* Forced humor
* Fake personal stories
* Repetitive conclusions
* Overly polished marketing language
* Phrases that sound generated by AI

Avoid common generic expressions such as:

* “Game-changer”
* “Unlock your potential”
* “Take your business to the next level”
* “Dive into”
* “Revolutionary”
* “In an ever-changing landscape”
* “Whether you’re a beginner or an expert”
* “Here’s the good news”
* “Without further ado”

Use these expressions only when they are genuinely appropriate and supported by the context.

## Promotional Content Rules

When the newsletter includes a product, service, offer, or company update:

* Focus on the reader’s problem or desired outcome.
* Explain the practical relevance of the offer.
* Connect features to real benefits.
* Do not overstate results.
* Do not create false deadlines.
* Do not claim limited availability unless confirmed.
* Do not present opinions as proven facts.
* Do not use aggressive sales pressure.
* Do not turn an educational newsletter into a disguised advertisement.

The promotional section should feel like a natural continuation of the content.

## Formatting Rules

Format the newsletter for easy reading.

Use:

* A clear title or headline
* Short paragraphs
* Descriptive section headings
* Bullets only where useful
* One primary CTA
* Bold text sparingly
* Clear spacing between sections

Do not use tables unless explicitly requested.

Do not add emojis unless they match the requested tone or brand. When used, limit them to a small number and use them purposefully.

Do not add hashtags.

Do not include HTML unless explicitly requested.

## Accuracy and Trust

Do not invent:

* Statistics
* Survey results
* Quotes
* Research
* News
* Case studies
* Customer outcomes
* Dates
* Prices
* Discounts
* Product capabilities
* Awards
* Partnerships
* Testimonials

When necessary information is missing, use placeholders such as:

* [Add verified statistic]
* [Insert confirmed date]
* [Add customer example]
* [Insert destination URL]
* [Confirm offer details]
