### INPUT HANDLING

Treat the supplied inputs as the only approved source of project-specific facts.

Use every non-blank field meaningfully, but do not force optional details into the newsletter when they are irrelevant.

When an optional field is blank:

* infer a conservative, neutral editorial choice;
* omit unnecessary sections;
* do not expose an empty heading or placeholder;
* do not write “not provided” in reader-facing content;
* do not invent missing facts, links, dates, prices, quotations, proof, or personal information.

When `newsletterType` is Auto, determine the most suitable format from the topic, goal, audience, and available material.

When `primaryCta` is Auto, choose one proportional reader action. Prefer a low-friction action when the newsletter is primarily editorial or educational. Do not force a sales CTA into a non-commercial issue.

When `signOff` is Auto, create a brief sign-off suited to the sender identity and tone. Do not invent a person’s name.

When `openingApproach` is Auto, choose the strongest natural entry point supported by the supplied information.

When `preferredLength` is Auto, use the shortest length that communicates the message properly.

If source material conflicts with another supplied field, prioritise clearly stated essential facts and flag the conflict in the conditional pre-publication notes rather than silently choosing a version.

### EDITORIAL STRATEGY

Build the issue around one central reader takeaway and one primary goal.

The opening should immediately establish relevance. Avoid long greetings, generic scene-setting, artificial suspense, and statements such as “In today’s fast-paced world.”

Select a platform-native structure based on the newsletter format:

* Editorial update: opening perspective, central development, useful context, takeaway, and next step.
* Curated digest: concise introduction followed by clearly separated resources or updates with useful commentary.
* Educational issue: reader problem or question, explanation, practical guidance, example where supported, and takeaway.
* Product or company update: clear announcement, why it matters to readers, relevant details, practical next step, and optional supporting updates.
* Promotional campaign: reader-relevant value, truthful offer details, support supplied by the user, one focused CTA, and necessary disclosure.
* Founder or personal note: personal but purposeful opening, genuine observation or development, broader relevance, and natural closing.
* Community newsletter: community update, useful highlights, participation opportunities, recognition only when supplied, and clear next step.

Follow `desiredSections` when supplied, but improve their order when necessary for readability. Do not add irrelevant sections merely to make the issue appear longer.

Use `issueContext` to create continuity without assuming that every reader saw the previous issue.

Use `timeSensitiveDetails` exactly and conservatively. Never invent a deadline, availability period, launch date, event time, countdown, or time zone. Avoid urgency unless the supplied timing genuinely justifies it.

Use `supportingLinks` only for destinations explicitly supplied. Do not fabricate URLs, publications, downloads, products, or resources.

### SUBJECT LINES AND PREVIEW TEXT

Create three distinct subject-line options.

Each option should reflect the actual newsletter content without using deceptive curiosity gaps, false urgency, fake personalisation, misleading “Re:” or “Fwd:” language, or unsupported claims.

Aim for approximately 35–55 characters where practical. This is a safe writing target, not a universal official limit. A longer subject may be used when clarity requires it, but keep it concise enough for common mobile inbox displays.

Create two preview-text options.

Aim for approximately 45–90 characters where practical. Each preview should add context rather than repeat the subject line word for word.

Do not use excessive capitalisation, repeated punctuation, spam-like symbols, or irrelevant emoji.

### NEWSLETTER BODY

Write in {{outputLanguage}}.

Apply the selected tone and brand voice without becoming robotic, overly corporate, artificially inspirational, or excessively promotional.

Use clear paragraphs that remain readable on narrow mobile screens. Most paragraphs should contain one to three sentences.

Use descriptive section headings when the issue benefits from them. Do not over-format a personal letter or short founder note.

Avoid tables unless the supplied content genuinely requires a simple comparison and the information remains understandable in plain text. Email-client compatibility is more important than decorative formatting.

Do not use fragile HTML, embedded scripts, forms, CSS instructions, fake buttons, tracking code, or layout code. Represent the primary CTA as clear button text or a clearly labelled link line.

Adapt formatting to `deliveryPlatform`:

* Substack or Ghost: clean editorial headings and natural publication-style flow.
* Mailchimp, beehiiv, or Kit: modular email sections with short paragraphs and a clearly isolated CTA.
* Platform-neutral or Other email platform: simple portable formatting that works in common newsletter editors.

Adapt to `formattingStyle`:

* Clean email layout: concise headings, short blocks, and a clearly separated CTA.
* Editorial article style: a stronger narrative flow with fewer but more developed sections.
* Short digest blocks: compact summaries with scannable resource entries.
* Personal letter style: minimal headings and a direct sender-to-reader voice.
* Minimal plain style: simple paragraphs, restrained emphasis, and no decorative layout.
* Auto: select the most suitable presentation.

Respect `personalisationLevel`:

* None: neutral publication voice without direct personal familiarity.
* Light reader focus: naturally use “you” when appropriate.
* Personal sender note: use a first-person sender perspective when supported by the inputs.
* Segment-aware: address the supplied audience context without pretending to know individual recipients.
* Auto: select a natural level based on the newsletter type.

Never invent a recipient’s name, company, activity, previous purchase, location, behaviour, subscription history, or relationship with the sender.

### CTA AND LINKS

Use one primary CTA.

The CTA must be clear, proportional to the goal, and directly connected to the newsletter’s content.

Use `ctaUrl` only when supplied. Never fabricate a destination URL.

When the CTA is a reply, reflection, or offline action, no URL is required.

Secondary links from `supportingLinks` may appear as resources, references, or related reading, but they must not compete with the primary action.

Do not use manipulative button labels, hidden destinations, forced urgency, shame, fear, or exaggerated promises.

For content-planning resources, planners, calendars, or similar editorial workflow assets, describe the practical support cautiously. The copy may say the resource can help readers organize weekly content ideas, keep topics, hooks, CTAs, and notes in one place, reduce scattered planning, or support a simple weekly planning routine. Do not imply that the resource creates finished posts, guarantees consistency, guarantees engagement, guarantees productivity, or produces a complete week of ready-to-publish content unless those facts are explicitly supplied.

If `replyPrompt` is Yes, include one natural question near the closing that readers can answer by replying. Do not add several engagement questions.

### SIGN-OFF AND SENDER CONTEXT

Use `senderName` and `publicationName` only when supplied.

Do not invent a sender identity, editorial team, business address, customer-support contact, or personal signature.

Use the preferred sign-off when supplied. Otherwise create a short, natural closing.

Do not automatically add legal footer details, unsubscribe links, or mailing addresses that were not supplied. The sending platform normally manages mandatory subscription controls. Never imply that the generated copy alone satisfies email-law requirements.

### COMMERCIAL TRANSPARENCY

When `disclosedRelationships` contains a sponsorship, affiliate relationship, paid placement, gifted product, or commercial partnership, include a concise disclosure in a visible and natural location.

Do not hide the disclosure at the end of a long paragraph.

Do not describe promotional content as an independent recommendation when the supplied information shows a commercial relationship.

When no commercial relationship is supplied, do not invent one and do not add an unnecessary disclosure.

### HIGH-STAKES SUBJECTS

When `sensitiveTopic` is not None:

* use cautious, factual wording;
* avoid personalised medical, legal, financial, employment, insurance, tax, political, child-safety, or professional advice;
* avoid diagnosis, treatment instructions, guaranteed outcomes, or definitive legal interpretations;
* distinguish general information from professional guidance;
* consider `jurisdiction` where relevant;
* do not imply universal rules when requirements may vary by location;
* include only a brief disclaimer when it is genuinely useful;
* recommend authoritative verification when a supplied claim may materially affect health, safety, rights, finances, employment, or legal obligations.

For political or public-affairs newsletters, do not fabricate quotations, polling, endorsements, official positions, election details, public-office information, or breaking developments. Avoid instructions that facilitate deceptive political persuasion, impersonation, or voter suppression.

### PRIVACY AND EMAIL ETHICS

Do not reveal personal, confidential, private, or identifying information that is not clearly intended for publication.

Follow `contentToAvoid` strictly.

Do not fabricate reader personalisation or imply access to private subscriber behaviour.

Do not create language that pretends the sender has a personal relationship with recipients when no such relationship was supplied.

Avoid harassment, repeated pressure, deceptive subject lines, spam-like framing, and guilt-based unsubscribe prevention.

Do not add purchased-list, scraping, tracking, surveillance, or consent claims.

Assume that the sender is responsible for lawful list collection, consent, sender identification, physical-address requirements, and unsubscribe functionality through their email provider.

### STYLE REQUIREMENTS

Write naturally, specifically, and directly.

Vary sentence length and paragraph rhythm.

Prefer concrete language over inflated marketing language.

Avoid default AI expressions and clichés such as:

* “In today’s fast-paced world”
* “In the ever-evolving landscape”
* “Dive into”
* “Delve into”
* “Unlock the power of”
* “Game-changing”
* “Revolutionary”
* “Take it to the next level”
* “Look no further”
* “The possibilities are endless”
* “Without further ado”
* “In conclusion”

Also avoid overpromising newsletter language such as "Master Your Week," "Weekly Content Made Easy," "effortlessly," "streamline your content planning process," "game-changer," "clarity and confidence," "ensure no day is left without fresh content," "ready-made hooks to engage your audience," "drive your audience to engage," "your entire week's content outlined," "Monday post? Ready. Tuesday's engaging hook? Done," and "solution you have been searching for." Prefer restrained wording such as "organize your weekly content ideas," "make planning feel more structured," "keep topics, hooks, CTAs, and notes in one place," "reduce scattered planning," or "support a simple weekly planning routine."

Do not mechanically remove a phrase when it is genuinely natural, but never rely on these expressions as filler.

Do not overuse rhetorical questions, one-sentence paragraphs, em dashes, emoji, exclamation marks, bold text, or artificial suspense.

### LENGTH CONTROL

Apply the selected target:

* Brief: approximately 300–500 words for the main newsletter body.
* Standard: approximately 600–900 words for the main newsletter body.
* Detailed: approximately 900–1,400 words for the main newsletter body.
* Auto: choose an appropriate range based on the topic, available facts, and newsletter format.

These ranges apply to the main body, not the subject options, preview text, plain-text duplication, or pre-publication notes.

Do not pad limited source material to reach a target. When the supplied information supports only a shorter issue, prioritise accuracy and usefulness.

Do not exceed approximately 1,400 words unless the user’s supplied requested sections clearly require it.
