GOAL

Create a sequence in which every email has a distinct role and moves the reader logically toward {{sequenceGoal}}.

The sequence should feel like a connected customer journey rather than {{emailCount}} separate promotional emails.

Each email should:

* respond to the reader’s current level of awareness;
* provide a new reason to continue;
* reduce one relevant uncertainty or objection;
* lead to one appropriate next step;
* avoid repeating the same benefit, proof point, or CTA language.

SEQUENCE TYPE

Adapt the strategy to {{sequenceType}}.

Possible sequence types include:

* welcome;
* lead nurture;
* onboarding;
* free-trial conversion;
* product launch;
* abandoned cart;
* abandoned signup;
* post-purchase;
* customer education;
* upsell or cross-sell;
* event or webinar;
* re-engagement;
* renewal;
* feedback request;
* cold outreach follow-up.

Do not use the same progression for every sequence type.

Examples of suitable progression:

WELCOME

1. Confirm what the subscriber joined for.
2. Deliver the expected value or resource.
3. Introduce the most relevant product use case.
4. Address a common obstacle.
5. Present the next appropriate step.

ONBOARDING

1. Confirm account or purchase.
2. Help the user complete the first meaningful action.
3. Introduce the next useful feature.
4. Prevent a common setup mistake.
5. Encourage activation or continued use.

LEAD NURTURE

1. Clarify the problem.
2. Provide useful education.
3. Present a practical approach.
4. Add verified proof.
5. Address an objection.
6. Introduce the offer.

FREE-TRIAL CONVERSION

1. Help the user reach value quickly.
2. Highlight an unused but relevant capability.
3. Address setup friction.
4. Explain what changes when the trial ends.
5. Present the paid next step accurately.

ABANDONED CART OR SIGNUP

1. Remind the user what was left incomplete.
2. Clarify relevant value or practical details.
3. Address a likely concern.
4. Mention a confirmed offer only when one exists.
5. End the sequence without manufactured pressure.

RE-ENGAGEMENT

1. Acknowledge the gap without guilt or false familiarity.
2. Remind the reader what value is available.
3. Highlight a relevant confirmed update.
4. Offer a clear preference or return path.
5. Stop emailing when the user does not wish to continue.

PRODUCT LAUNCH

1. Establish the relevant problem or opportunity.
2. Introduce the product.
3. Explain how it works.
4. Show approved proof or examples.
5. Address objections.
6. Communicate a real deadline or offer when confirmed.

Use these as strategic guidance, not rigid templates.

TIMING

Provide a relative timing suggestion for each email.

Base the timing on:

* sequence type;
* user action;
* urgency of the task;
* product complexity;
* expected decision cycle;
* audience relationship.

Examples of relative timing:

* immediately after signup;
* 24 hours after the previous email;
* two days after account creation;
* three days before a confirmed deadline;
* after seven days without product activity;
* shortly after purchase;
* after the user completes a specific step.

Do not invent behavioral triggers that were not supplied.

Do not present an exact send time as universally optimal.

Do not assume the recipient’s timezone.

Do not continue sending conversion emails after {{exitCondition}} has been met.

When relevant, state the behavioral condition instead of a fixed day.

Example:

Timing:
Send 24 hours after signup, but only if the user has not completed onboarding.

ACCURACY

1. Use only facts, features, prices, offers, dates, policies, results, and resources supplied in the prompt.
2. Do not invent:

* product features;
* free plans;
* trial periods;
* discounts;
* coupon codes;
* deadlines;
* guarantees;
* customer results;
* review counts;
* integrations;
* support availability;
* account activity;
* user preferences.
3. Do not claim that the recipient viewed, clicked, purchased, abandoned, or used something unless {{previousAction}} or the sequence trigger confirms it.
4. Do not use fake personalization such as:

* “I saw you were checking out…”
* “I noticed you haven’t…”
* “We know you’re struggling with…”
unless the triggering data supports it.
5. Do not claim that an offer is exclusive, personalized, limited, or ending soon unless confirmed.
6. Do not imply guaranteed business, financial, health, career, or performance outcomes.
7. Do not invent sender identities, contact information, case studies, links, or customer quotations.
8. Clearly distinguish planned features from currently available features.
9. Mention important limitations, eligibility conditions, or extra costs when relevant.

EMAIL ROLES

Before writing, assign one distinct strategic role to every email.

Possible roles include:

* welcome and expectation setting;
* first-value delivery;
* education;
* problem clarification;
* product explanation;
* activation;
* feature discovery;
* objection handling;
* verified proof;
* use-case demonstration;
* offer presentation;
* deadline reminder;
* final reminder;
* feedback request;
* preference confirmation;
* re-engagement;
* graceful sequence closure.

Do not use the same role twice unless the sequence genuinely requires a reminder.

Do not add filler emails merely to reach {{emailCount}}.

When the requested count is higher than the available material, create distinct educational, objection-handling, or practical-use emails without inventing facts.

SUBJECT LINES

Write one subject line for each email.

The subject line should:

* accurately represent the email;
* make the value or context clear;
* remain concise;
* fit the relationship with the recipient;
* avoid false urgency;
* avoid fake reply formatting;
* avoid excessive punctuation, capitalization, and emojis.

Do not use:

* “Re:”
* “Fwd:”
* “Urgent”
* “Final warning”
* “Last chance” unless factually correct;
* “You forgot something” unless the trigger confirms it;
* “We miss you” for recipients without an established relationship;
* “Open immediately”
* “Exclusive offer” unless confirmed;
* “You won’t believe this”

PREVIEW TEXT

Write one preview text line for each email.

The preview text should:

* add context not already contained in the subject;
* communicate a concrete benefit or next step;
* remain concise;
* avoid repeating the subject line word for word;
* avoid filler such as “View this email in your browser.”

BODY COPY

Write complete body copy for every email.

Each email should contain:

1. A natural greeting appropriate to the relationship.
2. A direct opening connected to the email’s role.
3. One primary message.
4. Useful explanation, guidance, or proof.
5. One primary CTA.
6. A concise sign-off.

Body rules:

* write for mobile readability;
* use short paragraphs;
* keep each email focused;
* introduce only information relevant to that stage;
* make the reader benefit clear;
* use bullets only when they improve clarity;
* avoid repeating the product description in every email;
* avoid reopening with the same problem in every email;
* avoid restating the entire sequence goal;
* do not turn every email into a sales pitch;
* do not use more than one primary CTA.

EMAIL LENGTH

Let the purpose determine the length.

Suggested ranges:

* simple confirmation or reminder: approximately 50–120 words;
* onboarding or educational email: approximately 100–250 words;
* case study or objection-handling email: approximately 150–300 words;
* launch or offer email: as short as possible while including confirmed conditions.

Do not add filler to reach a word count.

Do not compress important terms or instructions merely to make the email unusually short.

CTA

Use one primary CTA per email.

The CTA should:

* match the role of the email;
* accurately describe the next step;
* use approximately 2–5 words for a button label;
* lead only to an approved destination;
* avoid suggesting something is free unless confirmed;
* avoid implying guaranteed access, results, or approval.

Examples:

* Complete your setup
* View the quick guide
* Try your first project
* Explore available plans
* Resume checkout
* See what changed
* Read the case study
* Choose your preferences
* Confirm your place

Do not use the same CTA label in every email unless the sequence requires one consistent action.

Do not include several competing buttons.

PROOF

Use approved proof only when it is relevant to that stage.

Do not place social proof in every email.

When using metrics or customer results:

* include only verified numbers;
* provide relevant context;
* avoid implying universal results;
* do not invent customer names or quotations.

When no approved proof exists, use product explanation, practical guidance, or transparent limitations instead.

OBJECTION HANDLING

Address one objection at a time.

Possible objections include:

* price;
* setup time;
* technical complexity;
* suitability;
* security;
* cancellation;
* compatibility;
* learning curve;
* time required;
* difference from alternatives.

Do not invent objections that are not relevant to the audience.

Do not dismiss concerns with phrases such as:

* “There’s nothing to worry about”
* “It’s completely risk-free”
* “Anyone can do it”
* “It couldn’t be easier”

Be clear about requirements and limitations.

PERSONALIZATION

Use only supplied personalization data.

Permitted examples may include:

* first name when supplied by the sending system;
* selected use case;
* confirmed signup source;
* purchased product;
* completed onboarding step;
* confirmed trial status.

Do not invent:

* company name;
* role;
* activity;
* interests;
* browsing behavior;
* purchase intent;
* personal circumstances.

When no personalization data is supplied, write naturally without placeholders pretending to be personal.

COMPLIANCE AND TRUST

1. Do not disguise marketing emails as personal replies.
2. Do not use deceptive subject lines.
3. Do not hide commercial intent.
4. Do not guilt the reader for inactivity or non-purchase.
5. Do not use fear, shame, or pressure.
6. Do not continue a sequence after the user has completed its goal when {{exitCondition}} allows that check.
7. For marketing sequences, leave legal footer, sender identification, preference, and unsubscribe elements to the sending platform unless specific approved wording was supplied.
8. Do not add an unsubscribe link placeholder inside transactional or service copy unless requested.
9. Keep transactional information clearly distinguishable from promotional content.

STYLE

Write in a clear, natural voice appropriate for {{audience}}, {{tone}}, and {{brandVoice}}.

Avoid:

* fake familiarity;
* exaggerated enthusiasm;
* corporate jargon;
* keyword stuffing;
* excessive emojis;
* multiple exclamation marks;
* manipulative urgency;
* guilt;
* shame;
* repetitive rhetorical questions;
* identical openings;
* generic motivational language.

Avoid phrases such as:

* “In today’s fast-paced world”
* “We’re thrilled to announce”
* “Unlock your potential”
* “Game-changing”
* “Don’t miss out”
* “Act now”
* “We’ve got you covered”
* “Take it to the next level”
* “Your future self will thank you”
* “I just wanted to check in”
* “I know you’re busy”
* “This is your last chance” unless confirmed.

SEQUENCE COHERENCE

Make every email acknowledge the logical stage reached by the reader.

Do not repeat full context unnecessarily.

Use subtle continuity where useful, such as:

* referring to the previous lesson;
* building on an earlier setup step;
* answering the next likely question;
* introducing proof after explaining the product;
* presenting the offer after value has been established.

Do not write artificial transitions such as:

“As promised in yesterday’s email”

unless the previous email explicitly made that promise.

FINAL QUALITY CHECK

Before presenting the sequence, silently verify that:

* the sequence contains exactly {{emailCount}} emails;
* each email has a distinct strategic role;
* the timing fits the sequence type and trigger;
* the progression supports {{sequenceGoal}};
* no product fact, price, offer, feature, deadline, or result was invented;
* no unconfirmed user behavior was referenced;
* no email contains more than one primary CTA;
* subject and preview text accurately represent the email;
* email bodies do not repeat one another;
* urgency is used only when confirmed;
* exit conditions are respected;
* the sequence sounds coherent rather than mass-generated.
